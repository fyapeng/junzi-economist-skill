# Scarce Telemedicine Appointments and Access to Follow-Up Care: Evidence from Randomized Invitations

## Abstract

Can scarce telemedicine appointments improve access to specialty follow-up for rural patients who face high transportation barriers? We study the first three months of a regional platform serving 2,400 preregistered adults who had no household car, lived more than 30 km from specialty care, and had a clinician-documented need for follow-up. Appointment invitations were randomized at the individual level within clinician and week blocks. An invitation increased completion of clinically indicated follow-up within 30 days by 0.150 from a control mean of 0.44 (95% CI [0.101, 0.199]) and reduced round-trip travel time by 90 minutes from a control mean of 138 minutes (95% CI [-113.5, -66.5]). Bounds addressing missing travel-time reports preserve a reduction of at least 61 minutes. The invitation increased completed platform visits by 0.75, but 25 percent of invitees did not complete a platform visit; treatment-receipt interpretations require assumptions that may fail because invitation messages could affect scheduling directly. Ninety-day estimates for health, medical spending, and clinic exit are imprecise and do not establish improvement, savings, or equivalence. Observational saturation patterns also suggest possible displacement of noninvited patients, while clinician-week total follow-up did not increase precisely. The experiment therefore establishes that invitations improved invitees’ access and reduced travel, not that platform entry expanded systemwide access or raised net social welfare.

## Introduction

Telemedicine can relax a patient’s geographic constraint without relaxing the health system’s capacity constraint. That distinction is central when remote appointments are scarce. Giving a rural patient access to a virtual visit may eliminate a long trip and make clinically indicated follow-up more likely. Yet the same appointment can consume clinician time that would otherwise serve another patient, and priority conveyed by an invitation may change scheduling even if the recipient never uses the platform. An individual gain in access therefore need not represent a systemwide increase in care. The economic question is not simply whether telemedicine is available, but how scarce remote-care capacity reallocates follow-up across patients and changes the nonclinical costs of obtaining care.

This paper studies that question during the first three months of a regional telemedicine platform in rural counties. The study population consists of 2,400 preregistered adults selected before assignment on three dimensions of access need: they had no household car, lived more than 30 km from specialty care, and had a clinician-documented need for follow-up. Because the number of appointments was limited, invitations to book a platform appointment were randomized at the individual level within clinician and week blocks. Allocation logs and baseline balance were independently checked. This design creates a transparent comparison between invited and noninvited patients who faced similar underlying barriers and were attached to the same clinician and calendar week.

The randomized object is an invitation, not platform entry, treatment receipt, or county-level availability. That distinction governs the interpretation throughout the paper. Twenty-five percent of invitees did not complete a platform visit, and an invitation could affect behavior through more than the completed virtual encounter. The message may supply information, confer scheduling priority, prompt a patient to seek an in-person appointment, or induce administrative follow-up. The experiment thus identifies the intention-to-treat effect of offering an opportunity to book one of the scarce appointments under the implemented allocation rule. It does not, without additional assumptions, identify the effect of receiving telemedicine. Nor does it identify the effect of introducing the platform into a county, because all study participants were observed after entry and only invitations were randomized.

The first primary outcome is whether the patient completed clinically indicated follow-up within 30 days. In the control group, 44 percent completed follow-up. Invitation raised completion by 0.150, with a randomization-inference standard error of 0.025 and a 95 percent confidence interval of [0.101, 0.199]. Relative to the control mean, the point estimate represents an increase of roughly one third. The result shows that, for preregistered adults facing unusually high travel barriers, access to the invitation materially changed whether documented follow-up needs were met within a month. Because assignment was randomized within clinician-week blocks, this estimate is valid for assigned individuals under the implemented randomization even though outcomes elsewhere in the clinician’s panel may respond to the same capacity allocation.

The second primary outcome captures a different component of access: the travel burden required to obtain follow-up. Round-trip travel time includes zero travel for telemedicine. The control mean was 138 minutes. Invitation reduced travel by 90 minutes, with a randomization-inference standard error of 12 minutes and a 95 percent confidence interval of [-113.5, -66.5]. Missing travel time was limited and similar across arms—4 percent in treatment and 5 percent in control. Both inverse-probability adjustments and worst-case bounds preserve a reduction of at least 61 minutes. The travel result is therefore not merely an implication of more follow-up. It directly documents a large reduction in the time cost attached to obtaining care, while incorporating zero travel for completed remote visits.

These two outcomes reveal complementary margins. Follow-up completion measures whether indicated care occurred; travel time measures a resource cost borne by the patient in reaching that care. An invitation may improve the first margin through information or scheduling priority and the second through substitution from in-person to remote encounters. The experiment does not isolate these mechanisms, but reduced travel itself is directly observed. This matters because a platform can generate value for patients even when short-run clinical outcomes do not move detectably: it can lower the time and transportation burden of a visit that would otherwise occur. Conversely, substitution toward remote care could reduce travel without increasing the total amount of care delivered. Reporting both outcomes prevents either interpretation from being imposed on the data alone.

The secondary outcomes do not establish downstream health improvement or financial savings. At 90 days, the estimated effect on a standardized health index is 0.010 standard deviations, with a standard error of 0.030 and a 95 percent confidence interval of [-0.049, 0.069]. The estimate for total medical spending is +$20, with a standard error of $45 and a 95 percent confidence interval of [-$68, $108]. The estimate for clinic exit is -0.003, with a standard error of 0.006 and a 95 percent confidence interval of [-0.015, 0.009]. These intervals include both favorable and unfavorable values of economically relevant size. They are imprecise evidence, not equivalence tests. The appropriate conclusion is that the study detects no precise short-run change in these outcomes, not that invitation left health, spending, or clinic attachment unchanged.

Noncompliance further limits what can be learned about treatment receipt. Invitation increased completed platform visits by 0.75. Dividing the 0.150 intention-to-treat effect on follow-up by this first stage produces a Wald ratio of 0.20. A causal interpretation of that ratio as the effect of a completed platform visit for compliers would require exclusion, monotonicity, and no interference. Exclusion is not guaranteed: an invitation message could change scheduling behavior even for a patient who never completes a virtual visit. Shared clinicians also make a simple no-interference assumption doubtful. We therefore treat 0.20 as an arithmetic ratio rather than a treatment-effect estimate. The experimentally supported result remains the effect of invitation under the allocation mechanism actually used.

Capacity is the main reason that the individual estimates cannot be translated directly into systemwide welfare. Among other preregistered patients assigned to the same clinician but not invited, follow-up was 0.050 lower, with a standard error of 0.020, relative to comparable clinician-weeks with lower invitation saturation. At the clinician-week level, total completed indicated follow-up rose by 0.020 per eligible patient, with a standard error of 0.018 and a 95 percent confidence interval of [-0.015, 0.055]. These patterns are consistent with some displacement: invitations may have shifted scarce attention toward recipients without generating a comparably clear increase in total completed follow-up. But invitation saturation was not randomized. Clinician-weeks with higher saturation may also have differed in demand or staffing, so the spillover estimates are observational. They motivate a capacity interpretation; they do not identify displacement causally or prove that total access was unchanged.

The distinction between randomized assignment and observational saturation produces a sharper reading of the evidence. The invitation effect answers what happened to assigned patients when scarce opportunities were offered according to the experimental rule. It remains an internally valid comparison for invitees even if noninvitees were affected. A social-welfare calculation, however, must include both groups and the resources used to serve them. Relevant benefits include completed indicated care, lower travel time and cost, convenience, and possible health gains. Relevant costs include platform and clinician resources, displaced care, patient time, public spending, and distributional consequences across invited and noninvited patients. The experiment measures only part of this ledger. It establishes gains in invitees’ follow-up and travel margins, but it does not establish positive net social welfare, systemwide access expansion, health improvement, or spending savings.

The findings speak to a broader allocation problem in digital service delivery. A technology that reduces a user’s cost of accessing a professional need not increase the professional’s capacity. When demand exceeds available appointments, the allocation rule becomes part of the intervention. Invitations can determine who receives information, who is prioritized, and who bears travel costs; their effects may operate even when take-up is incomplete. Evaluations that compare invited with noninvited users can therefore identify the effect of an offer while leaving unresolved whether the technology creates additional service capacity or redistributes existing capacity. The present experiment makes that boundary visible because randomization occurred at the individual level inside shared clinician-week markets.

The paper contributes evidence on two objects that should remain distinct. First, it quantifies how randomized invitations to scarce remote-care appointments affect realized access among rural adults with documented follow-up needs and high transportation barriers. The combination of a 0.150 increase in timely follow-up and a 90-minute reduction in travel shows that an invitation can change both utilization and the cost of reaching care. Second, it documents why those individual effects are insufficient for a system-level conclusion. Incomplete take-up, possible direct effects of invitation messages, shared clinicians, and nonrandom saturation prevent a clean treatment-receipt or welfare interpretation. Relevant literature on telemedicine access, rationing under capacity constraints, noncompliance, and interference should be used to position these contributions, but no citations are inserted here because the supplied evidence packet contains no verified bibliography.

External validity is similarly bounded. The sample was preregistered, had no household car, lived more than 30 km from specialty care, and already had a clinician-documented need for follow-up. These conditions select patients for whom avoiding travel and facilitating scheduling could be especially consequential. The estimates do not describe residents with easier transportation, those living closer to specialty care, patients without a documented follow-up need, or populations exposed to a mature platform with different capacity. They also describe the first three months of operation, when workflows, staffing, and patient familiarity may differ from a steady state. The study is informative precisely because it identifies a clearly defined allocation effect in a high-barrier population; extending that result beyond the experimental population requires additional evidence.

Finally, an exploratory subgroup result should not alter the paper’s main interpretation. In a diabetes subgroup chosen after viewing the results, the follow-up intention-to-treat estimate is +0.25 with an unadjusted p-value of 0.03. Because the subgroup was selected post hoc, no multiplicity adjustment was applied, and no independent replication exists, this pattern is only a hypothesis for future prespecified testing. It is not evidence that invitations are more effective for patients with diabetes. The confirmatory conclusions remain the prespecified invitation effects on follow-up completion and travel, together with the explicitly limited evidence on downstream outcomes and capacity.

Taken together, the results show that randomized invitations to scarce telemedicine appointments improved access for the patients who received them. The gain is visible in a higher probability of timely indicated follow-up and a substantial reduction in travel time. What the results do not show is equally important: they do not isolate the effect of a completed virtual visit, demonstrate clinical quality, establish health or spending gains, or prove that the platform expanded total access. Scarcity converts telemedicine from a purely technological intervention into an allocation problem. Evaluating it requires attention not only to the patients offered remote care, but also to the patients and resources on the other side of the capacity constraint.
