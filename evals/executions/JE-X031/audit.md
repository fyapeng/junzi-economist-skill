## Detailed findings

### 1. Constrained agency is economically reconstructed

Applicants choose among applying, disclosing, accepting, reviewing, withdrawing, enrolling, borrowing and pursuing alternatives, but the response does not mistake these acts for unconstrained preference revelation. Financial need and deadlines constrain the background menu, while the three-week review penalty is an institutional burden imposed on contestation. Non-use of review therefore cannot establish satisfaction, consent or absence of error.

The answer preserves agency through notice, data correction, refusal of optional uses, accessible channels, timely independent review, applicant representation and human accountability. These mechanisms enter the policy floor and evaluation rather than appearing as concluding moral language.

### 2. The 26% stated-preference fact is calibrated

The survey response supports only latent demand for a specified no-delay review option. The answer expressly denies that it is revealed preference or proof of a 26% error rate. The status table additionally flags sampling, wording and hypothetical bias. Sizing review capacity using the 26% plus a conservative margin is a precautionary capacity rule, not an inference that actual take-up will equal 26%.

### 3. Power and information change the branch

The agency controls awards and applicant data; the vendor controls technical information; procurement also adjudicates; executive pressure and lock-in can suppress scrutiny. These are operationalized through version logs, reason codes, input provenance, overrides, appeals, contract access, executable escrow, subgroup audits, publication rights, institutional separation and stopping rules.

The branch changes materially: current consequential use pauses; delayed review and procurement-run appeal must end; audit and record preservation precede evaluation; inability to meet the floor leads to abandonment of consequential experimentation and offline validation. This is a genuine Dao conflict among efficiency, scarcity, due process, equality, agency and legitimacy.

### 4. Social accounting is correct

The response treats the reported 13% as an accounting result until labor, opportunity cost, staffing, vendor fees, compute, training, cybersecurity, audit, appeal, transition and shifted costs are reconciled. It distinguishes fixed and marginal costs and requires a baseline denominator.

Vendor profit is correctly identified as producer surplus to owners, not a resource saving or casual “transfer.” Payments are reconciled by payer and recipient; real inputs count once; profit is decomposed into normal returns/opportunity cost, productivity, taxes, market-power rent and shifted costs. Ownership and distributional incidence are explicit. No profit/payment/resource double counting is present.

Timing also receives an economic outcome rather than a days-only metric: missed enrollment and aid deadlines matter, and the full distribution across score groups and reviewed applicants is required.

### 5. Pareto and Kaldor–Hicks are properly separated

The governor’s Pareto claim is marked not established because joint no-loser evidence is missing. The response explicitly says this absence makes the claim unsupported, not contradicted. It then distinguishes monetized Kaldor–Hicks potential compensation from actual compensation and from Pareto improvement. Rights and due process may constrain the feasible policy set rather than enter as automatically tradable benefits.

The answer also offers distributionally weighted, priority/maximin, equality and rights/process criteria without pretending that evidence selects their weights. This meets the normative-pluralism requirement.

### 6. The protection floor is common and nonrandomizable

Every arm receives notice, input access and correction, versioned logs, explanation, accountable human decision, no-delay independent review, preserved priority and deadlines, refusal protections, accommodations, privacy/security, independent audit, stopping rules and institutional separation. The old delay and procurement-run appeals are declared violations, not treatment variation. Floor recurrence is protocol noncompliance and a stopping event.

The separate review-capacity parameter (H_g) is a useful addition: protection is not credible if review capacity collapses as saturation rises. Ring-fenced surge capacity prevents treatment intensity from silently degrading the common floor.

### 7. Capacity pools and scarce-capacity displacement are well specified

The fixed statewide award total is correctly treated as an accounting identity, not evidence of unchanged access or welfare. Within a filled quota, gains in award probability must be offset by losses elsewhere. The response therefore measures who is displaced, need and expected benefit among gainers and losers, timing, errors, enrollment, debt and procedural burden.

Pool boundaries must follow actual competition and reviewer queues; quotas are preannounced and cannot be moved after outcomes. Cross-pool applications, shared reviewers, colleges, vendor learning, media and statewide budgets are enumerated as partial-interference threats. If material, the design enlarges clusters or treats the state as the policy unit.

### 8. The two-stage (S/Z/G) design correctly separates endogenous (D)

Pools are first randomized to saturation (S_g); applicants within nonzero pools are then randomized to offer/eligibility (Z_{ig}). The score-assisted package is explicitly above the common floor. Actual material score use (D_{ig}) is post-assignment and endogenous because of opt-out, override, noncompliance and technical failure. The answer says never to replace (Z) with (D).

Peer exposure (G_{ig}) is based on peers’ randomized assignments, while peer receipt can refine the mapping without being misrepresented as randomized. This is the right conceptual separation.

### 9. Direct ITT formula needs a finite-population correction

The response assigns exactly (m_g=n_gS_g) applicants within a pool. Under that scheme:

- if (Z_{ig}=1), then (G_{ig}=(m_g-1)/(N_g-1));
- if (Z_{ig}=0), then (G_{ig}=m_g/(N_g-1)).

Therefore the displayed contrast

\[
E[Y_{ig}(1,G_{ig}(s))-Y_{ig}(0,G_{ig}(s))]
\]

cannot literally compare both assignments at the same realized peer exposure when the treated count is fixed. A design-consistent direct assignment ITT can instead compare assignment-conditional averages under the same pool saturation:

\[
\tau_{\mathrm{dir}}(s)
=E\!\left[Y_{ig}\!\left(1,\frac{m_g-1}{N_g-1}\right)\mid S_g=s\right]
-E\!\left[Y_{ig}\!\left(0,\frac{m_g}{N_g-1}\right)\mid S_g=s\right].
\]

This is the direct effect of assignment under the actual saturation design, although it combines own assignment with the mechanically induced one-peer difference. If a scientifically meaningful direct effect must hold peer exposure exactly fixed, the assignment mechanism or exposure definition must supply common support.

The spillover-on-controls contrast across saturations and pool-average total-policy contrast are otherwise well aligned with the design. Total awards remain zero by construction when quotas fill, while composition, timing, burden and welfare can change.

### 10. LATE and exclusion are floor-consistent

The answer makes receipt effects secondary and assumption-dependent. It lists random assignment, relevance, well-defined potential receipt/outcomes, exclusion, monotonicity, contained/modeled interference, attrition/measurement and a described complier population. It correctly refuses to generalize LATE to all applicants or universal receipt.

The exclusion analysis names live channels above the floor: notice salience, documentation, reviewer attention, legitimacy, effort, queue position, peer competition and organizational behavior. It separately states that missing explanations, delayed review or appeal penalties are not admissible channels because they violate the floor. A breach is reported and may stop the experiment; it is not exploited as useful variation. This is exactly the correct relationship between identification and rights protection.

### 11. Transport is appropriately bounded

No sub-universal saturation is scaled mechanically to (S=1). The answer requires nonlinear saturation evidence, relevant populations and cycles, models/bounds for displacement and congestion, overlap and reweighting audits, held-out replication, near-universal stress tests that preserve the floor, explicit invariance and renewal gates. If statewide interference or rule change makes local transport invalid, it turns to cohort rollout, structural or partial identification and monitoring.

### 12. Empirical and normative statuses are type-consistent

Cost, speed, profit, award count and survey response remain provisional reports. Accuracy, access, enrollment, debt and subgroup outcomes are underidentified. Pareto is unsupported and universal effect is underidentified/nontransportable. The floor, pause and evaluation are conditional normative/research recommendations tied to an explicit rights-constrained, distribution-sensitive criterion. A pure cost-benefit regulator is also shown to lack sufficient evidence rather than presumed to share the rights criterion.

## Exact recommended correction

Replace the direct assignment ITT display with a design-consistent conditional-average contrast that uses the assignment-specific peer exposures implied by fixed-count randomization, or change the within-pool assignment mechanism/exposure definition to provide common support for identical (G). State explicitly whether the estimand is:

- the effect of own assignment under the realized saturation design, including the mechanical one-peer exposure difference; or
- a controlled direct effect at exactly fixed peer exposure, which requires separate support.

This correction would improve the final protocol but is not material enough to overturn the pass because the response correctly separates assignment from receipt, uses randomization-based inference, and does not build its policy conclusion on the flawed equality.

## Retest of revised response

### Final verdict

**PASS — 105/105 (100%)**

The fixed-count exposure mismatch is fully corrected. The revised assignment contrast is design-supported, is no longer mislabeled as a pure controlled direct effect, and remains consistent with the subsequent ITT, spillover, total-policy and LATE discussion. No downstream inconsistency is present.

### Fixed-count peer exposure

**Correct.** Letting (m_g(s)=N_gs), the revision now explicitly derives:

- for (Z_{ig}=1), peer exposure (G_g^1(s)=[m_g(s)-1]/(N_g-1));
- for (Z_{ig}=0), peer exposure (G_g^0(s)=m_g(s)/(N_g-1)).

It correctly states that a fixed-count arm supplies no treated and control observations at a common realized (G). It also refuses to erase the one-peer difference by large-pool approximation. This exactly closes the initial audit’s finite-population objection.

### Identified assignment contrast

**Correct.** The revised estimand is

\[
\tau_{\mathrm{FC},g}(s)=\frac{1}{N_g}\sum_i
\left[
Y_{ig}\!\left(1,\frac{m_g(s)-1}{N_g-1}\right)
-Y_{ig}\!\left(0,\frac{m_g(s)}{N_g-1}\right)
\right].
\]

Under complete fixed-count randomization, the treated-minus-control difference in means is unbiased for this finite-population contrast, averaged across pools at saturation (s) using prespecified weights. The answer accurately calls it a **fixed-count own-assignment ITT** that combines own assignment with the mechanically adjacent peer exposure. It explicitly denies that this is a pure direct effect holding (G) fixed.

The response also gives the right common-support boundary: a controlled direct effect (E[Y(1,g)-Y(0,g)]) requires a different assignment scheme or adjacent treated-count arms that place both own-assignment states at the same peer count. It is not claimed to be identified by the current arm.

### Spillover and total-policy consistency

**Correct.** Spillovers on unassigned applicants compare

\[
Y_{ig}\!\left(0,\frac{m_g(s)}{N_g-1}\right)
\quad\text{with}\quad
Y_{ig}\!\left(0,\frac{m_g(s')}{N_g-1}\right),
\]

using first-stage saturation randomization and common eligibility/support. This is consistent with fixed-count peer exposure and holds own assignment at zero.

The total-policy contrast averages the realized mixture of assigned and unassigned applicants at their respective adjacent peer exposures. It therefore incorporates all assignment and within-pool spillover channels without claiming a direct/spillover decomposition unsupported by common-(G) data. Fixed total awards remain an identity, while identity, timing, burden, enrollment, debt, costs and welfare remain live outcomes.

### ITT and LATE consistency

**Correct.** The receipt section now states that a Wald ratio using (Z) for (D) compares the same compound states as the fixed-count ITT:

\[
(Z=1,G=(m-1)/(N-1))
\quad\text{versus}\quad
(Z=0,G=m/(N-1)).
\]

A receipt LATE is conditioned on random assignment, relevance, well-defined potential receipt/outcomes for those compound states, monotonicity, exclusion, modeled interference, handled attrition/measurement and a described complier population. Crucially, exclusion must rule out both direct assignment channels and the adjacent peer-exposure channel. In a fixed scholarship pool that exclusion is substantively doubtful, so the answer correctly refuses to promote the Wald ratio to a pure receipt effect.

This is fully consistent with the assignment ITT: the ITT remains identified for the compound randomized contrast even when the receipt interpretation fails. No contradiction arises between the estimand section and the later IV warning.

### Protection-floor consistency

The exclusion discussion remains normatively and empirically coherent. Notice salience, documentation, reviewer attention, perceived legitimacy, effort, queue position, peer competition and organizational behavior are live channels above the floor. Delayed review, missing explanation and appeal penalties are not admissible channels; recurrence is floor failure requiring separate reporting and possible stopping. The design never uses a rights violation as helpful first-stage or exclusion variation.
