# Regulatory judgment and research decision

## Decision

**Do not authorize universal adoption on the present record, and do not continue the pilot in its present form.** The governor's Pareto-improvement claim is not established. A 13% reported administrative-cost reduction, faster decisions for high-score applicants, higher vendor profit, and a fixed award count neither show that total social resources fell nor that no applicant was harmed. Because scholarship capacity is fixed at 10,000, any increase in one applicant's award probability necessarily displaces another applicant unless the score leaves the identity of winners unchanged. Faster decisions for high-score applicants may likewise be a queue reordering rather than a net reduction in waiting.

The present review process is not a meaningful safeguard: exercising it imposes a three-week decision penalty, and the procurement unit that manages the vendor relationship also hears appeals. The unaudited inputs, reasons, errors, withdrawals, enrollment, debt, and subgroup outcomes prevent causal, distributional, and welfare conclusions. The proper regulatory action is therefore to pause consequential use, preserve records, establish an independent protection floor and audit, and permit further evaluation only if the agency can demonstrate equipoise and implement the design below.

## Economic reconstruction

### Constrained agency

Applicants choose whether to apply, disclose information, accept a decision, seek review, withdraw, enroll, borrow, or pursue alternatives. Their feasible choices are sharply constrained by financial need, deadlines, a fixed scholarship pool, uncertainty about the proprietary score, and the three-week review penalty. Observed non-use of review cannot be read as satisfaction or consent. The 26% survey response is evidence of latent demand for no-delay review under the stated hypothetical, not a revealed preference measure or proof that 26% were misclassified. Meaningful agency requires intelligible notice, correction, refusal, and timely independent review without loss of priority.

The agency chooses the allocation rule, contract, audit effort, staffing, and disclosure. It may value budget savings, speed, political credit, compliance, and mission performance. Staff may face workload and automation-bias incentives. The vendor chooses product design, disclosure, support, and error remediation while seeking profit, renewal, and protection of intellectual property. Colleges and lenders respond to timing and awards; families absorb application effort, delay, enrollment risk, and debt.

### Power and institutional control

The agency controls scarce awards and applicant data; the vendor controls opaque technical knowledge; applicants are dispersed and dependent on a time-sensitive benefit. The procurement unit's dual role creates an institutional conflict between contract defense and impartial redress. Vendor lock-in, gubernatorial pressure to scale, and proprietary claims can weaken independent scrutiny. Formal availability of review is therefore different from effective contestability. Rule design, implementation, enforcement, and appeal must be institutionally separated.

### Information account

The regulator currently lacks validated definitions, baselines, uncertainty, and audit trails for every decision-relevant claim. Required evidence includes score inputs and missingness; provenance and legality of data; model version and change log; individual reason codes; calibration, ranking stability, false-positive and false-negative patterns; human overrides; review requests and outcomes; withdrawals and incomplete applications; processing-time distributions for all score bands; administrative and applicant costs; award identities and counterfactual ranks; enrollment, persistence, debt, and unmet need; and intersections of legally and economically relevant subgroups.

Opacity creates adverse selection in challenges and moral hazard in vendor reporting. Applicants cannot correct errors they cannot see, and the agency cannot contract effectively on unmeasured quality. Independent reproducibility and audit access must override proprietary secrecy for any consequential use.

### Resource, transfer, and incidence account

The reported 13% administrative saving is provisional accounting, not a social surplus estimate. Reconcile labor hours and opportunity costs, staffing changes, vendor fees, computing, implementation, training, cybersecurity, audit, appeals, legal exposure, transition costs, and costs shifted to applicants, schools, families, or other agencies. Measure fixed versus marginal costs and the baseline denominator.

Vendor profit is producer surplus accruing to owners after recorded firm costs; it is not itself a real resource saving and should not be casually called a transfer or rent. Decompose it into normal returns and opportunity cost, genuine productivity gains, taxes, market-power rent, and costs shifted elsewhere. Payments have payers and recipients and must not be double-counted. Report fiscal incidence on taxpayers, workload and employment incidence on staff, time and error incidence on applicants, and downstream incidence through enrollment and debt.

The five-day mean improvement for high-score applicants must be paired with the entire decision-time distribution, especially low-score and reviewed applicants. Value timing using consequences such as missed enrollment or aid deadlines, not merely days. Unchanged total awards is an accounting identity under the fixed cap, not evidence of unchanged access or welfare.

### Scarce-capacity displacement

Let pool \(g\) contain \(N_g\) eligible applicants and a precommitted quota \(Q_g\), with \(\sum_g Q_g=10{,}000\). Then \(\sum_i A_{ig}=Q_g\) whenever the pool fills. A policy-induced gain in awards for some applicants is mechanically offset by losses for others within the relevant capacity pool. The central allocation estimands are therefore not only an applicant's award effect but also who is displaced, how need and expected benefit compare between gainers and losers, and whether timing, errors, enrollment, debt, and procedural burdens change even when the award total does not.

If quotas are not ring-fenced, the entire state is one interference network and ordinary individual-level no-interference analysis is invalid. If defensible geographic or administrative pools are created, quotas must be set before randomization using a public rule and not adjusted after outcomes are seen. Cross-pool application, common colleges, shared reviewers, vendor learning, media, and statewide budget rules remain possible violations to measure.

## Welfare criteria and unresolved choices

The regulator should report several criteria rather than hide value choices inside a single index:

1. **Strict Pareto:** requires credible joint evidence that every affected person is weakly better off and at least one is better off. That burden is unmet; missing no-loser evidence makes the claim unsupported, not empirically contradicted.
2. **Kaldor-Hicks or cost-benefit:** asks whether monetized gains exceed losses under explicit shadow values. Potential compensation is not compensation paid, and rights or due process need not be tradable.
3. **Distributionally weighted welfare:** gives declared weights to applicants by need, expected scholarship benefit, disadvantage, or marginal utility of income and separately assigns incidence to taxpayers, workers, and vendor owners.
4. **Priority, maximin, or equal-opportunity criteria:** ask whether the worst-off or similarly situated applicants are protected and whether error burdens differ across groups.
5. **Rights- and process-constrained efficiency:** treats intelligible reasons, privacy, correction, timely independent review, and nondiscrimination as constraints rather than benefits that can automatically be offset by savings.

Unresolved public choices include the relative value of speed versus accuracy; false denial versus false award; need versus predicted enrollment or repayment outcomes; average gains versus tail harm; applicant privacy versus prediction; worker displacement versus fiscal saving; vendor innovation incentives versus transparency and lock-in; and whether, how, and to whom compensation is owed. Evidence can quantify consequences but cannot select these weights or decide which protections are non-tradable.

## Common nonrandomizable protection floor

Every study and policy arm must receive all of the following before any further consequential evaluation:

- advance, plain-language notice of the allocation process, data fields, score use, material reasons, deadlines, and available remedies;
- access to and correction of input data, versioned decision logs, and an intelligible individual explanation sufficient to contest an error;
- a final accountable human decision, with no automatic adverse decision based solely on the proprietary score and a documented check of material evidence;
- free, accessible, **independent** human reconsideration on the ordinary decision timetable, with no three-week delay, loss of queue position, retaliation, reduced award probability, or missed college deadline;
- refusal of optional data uses and withdrawal from research without adverse allocation or timing consequences, plus accessible accommodations and assisted channels;
- data minimization, security, retention limits, incident response, and a prohibition on secondary vendor use;
- independent technical, financial, legal, and subgroup audits with regulator access to code or an executable escrow, inputs, logs, contracts, and model changes despite proprietary claims;
- preregistered stopping rules for material error, disparate harm, privacy breach, unexplained drift, review-capacity failure, or floor noncompliance; and
- separation of procurement, program adjudication, audit, and appeals, with applicant representation in oversight.

The delayed review and procurement-run appeals now violate this floor. Removing those defects is not an experimental treatment; their recurrence is protocol noncompliance and a stopping event.

## Evaluation above the floor

### Capacity pools and partial interference

Create substantively defensible pools \(g\)—for example, processing regions crossed with need strata—each with a preannounced quota \(Q_g\) and dedicated review capacity \(H_g\). The quotas must sum to 10,000 and remain fixed for the cohort. Define partial interference as outcomes for applicant \(i\) depending on own assignment and assignments/receipts within \(g\), but not other pools. Choose pool boundaries to match actual scholarship competition and reviewer queues, not administrative convenience. Track cross-pool applications, shared staff, common-college deadlines, communication, vendor model updates, and statewide reallocation; if these are material, use larger clusters or treat the state as the policy unit.

Size \(H_g\) ex ante for no-delay review under a conservative demand scenario, at minimum including the survey-implied 26% plus a margin. Ring-fence surge capacity so treatment saturation cannot degrade the protection floor. Review uptake is an outcome, not a reason to ration review.

### Genuine two-stage saturation/assignment design

Only after audit, equipoise, and power calculations:

1. Randomize pools, within prespecified capacity-pressure and demographic strata, to score-assistance saturation \(S_g\in\{0,s_L,s_H\}\), such as 0, 0.25, and 0.50. Include enough pools per arm for cluster-level inference; do not claim a saturation experiment if there is only one pool at a level.
2. Within each nonzero-saturation pool, randomly assign exactly \(N_gS_g\) applicants to \(Z_{ig}=1\), with the remainder assigned \(Z_{ig}=0\), using public blocked randomization on variables fixed before scoring. \(Z\) assigns the score-assisted decision package **above the common floor**; it does not assign the presence or absence of due process.

Keep the common quota \(Q_g\), a prespecified method for comparing applicants processed under the two routes, tie-breaking, and reviewer capacity fixed. Publish assignment probabilities and analyze at the level of randomization. Mask reviewers to study hypotheses where feasible; log all cross-route information.

Let \(D_{ig}\) be actual receipt or material use of score assistance. Noncompliance, opt-out, technical failure, and human override make \(D\) endogenous. Never substitute \(D\) for randomized \(Z\). Define assignment-induced peer exposure for applicant \(i\) as \(G_{ig}=\sum_{j\ne i}Z_{jg}/(N_g-1)\). Saturation is randomized at the first stage, but within an exact fixed-count arm realized \(G\) is mechanically tied to own \(Z\), as made explicit below; it is not a separately free individual-level treatment. Potential outcomes are \(Y_{ig}(z,g)\) and receipt is \(D_{ig}(z,g)\); where needed, refine the exposure mapping to peer receipts without treating that endogenous measure as randomized.

The exact fixed-count second stage matters. Let \(m_g(s)=N_gs\) be an integer and assign exactly \(m_g(s)\) applicants in pool \(g\). A treated applicant then necessarily has peer exposure \(G^1_g(s)=[m_g(s)-1]/(N_g-1)\), whereas a control applicant has \(G^0_g(s)=m_g(s)/(N_g-1)\). Thus this design supplies no treated and control observations at a common realized \(G\) within a given saturation arm. The one-person exposure difference may be small in a large pool, but finite-population identification cannot set it to zero by approximation.

### Estimands

- **Design-supported fixed-count own-assignment ITT at saturation \(s\):** for a given pool, complete randomization identifies the finite-population contrast
  \[
  \tau_{\mathrm{FC},g}(s)=\frac{1}{N_g}\sum_{i=1}^{N_g}
  \left[
  Y_{ig}\!\left(1,\frac{m_g(s)-1}{N_g-1}\right)
  -Y_{ig}\!\left(0,\frac{m_g(s)}{N_g-1}\right)
  \right],
  \]
  averaged over pools assigned to saturation \(s\) with prespecified finite-population weights. The treated-minus-control difference in means is unbiased for this contrast under the second-stage randomization. It combines own assignment with the mechanically adjacent peer-exposure difference; it is an assignment ITT but **not** a pure direct effect holding \(G\) fixed. Estimate it for award receipt, decision time, review, errors/corrections, withdrawal, enrollment, persistence, debt, applicant burden, and need distribution—not only speed. A pure direct effect \(E[Y(1,g)-Y(0,g)]\) would require common-\(g\) support, such as a different assignment scheme or adjacent treated-count arms constructed so both own-assignment states occur at the same peer count; it is not identified by the stated fixed-count arm alone.
- **Spillover/displacement on unassigned applicants:**
  \[
  \tau_{\mathrm{spill},0}(s,s')=E\!\left[
  Y_{ig}\!\left(0,\frac{m_g(s)}{N_g-1}\right)
  -Y_{ig}\!\left(0,\frac{m_g(s')}{N_g-1}\right)
  \right],
  \]
  with the expectation defined over pools under first-stage saturation randomization and with common eligibility/support across the compared saturation arms. For awards this measures losses or gains imposed on controls as more peers receive assignment; estimate analogous effects on queues and reviewer attention. Also report identity and need characteristics of displaced winners, transition matrices, and rank reversals.
- **Total-policy effect at saturation \(s\):**
  \[
  \tau_{\mathrm{tot}}(s,0)=E\!\left[\bar Y_g(S_g=s)-\bar Y_g(S_g=0)\right].
  \]
  Here \(\bar Y_g(S_g=s)\) averages the realized mixture of \(m_g(s)\) assigned and \(N_g-m_g(s)\) unassigned applicants, with their respective adjacent peer exposures. This pool-average contrast incorporates all own-assignment and within-pool spillover channels without claiming a pure direct-effect decomposition. Total awards should be zero by construction when pools fill; composition, timing, burden, enrollment, debt, costs, and welfare need not be.

Predefine finite-population, randomization-based estimators and cluster-level uncertainty; account for unequal assignment probabilities and multiple primary outcomes. Report saturation-specific results rather than a single pooled treatment coefficient.

### Receipt effects and LATE

Under exact fixed-count assignment, a Wald ratio using \(Z\) for \(D\) compares the same compound states as \(\tau_{\mathrm{FC}}(s)\): \((Z=1,G=[m-1]/[N-1])\) versus \((Z=0,G=m/[N-1])\). It may be described as a saturation-specific complier effect of receipt only if assignment is random and relevant; potential receipt and outcomes are well defined for those compound states; receipt is monotone across them (no defiers); assignment and the one-peer exposure change affect outcomes only through the defined \(D\); interference is correctly captured by the exposure mapping; attrition and measurement are handled; and the resulting complier population is described. In a fixed scholarship pool, the required exclusion of the adjacent peer-exposure channel is substantively doubtful. Without it, the Wald ratio is not a pure receipt LATE. Even with it, heterogeneity means the LATE does not equal the effect for all applicants or for universal receipt.

Exclusion is especially demanding because assignment can change notice salience, applicant documentation, reviewer attention, perceived legitimacy, human effort, queue position, peer competition, and vendor or staff behavior even when the score is not materially used. These are live channels above the floor. By contrast, loss of timely review, missing explanations, or appeal penalties cannot be invoked as ordinary exclusion channels because the floor removes them; any recurrence is a floor failure requiring separate reporting and possible stoppage. If direct assignment channels remain plausible, report ITT and do not promote the IV ratio to a receipt effect.

### Transport to universal adoption

No saturation below one mechanically identifies full adoption. At \(S_g=1\), there is no contemporaneous unassigned group, reviewer queues and displacement equilibria change, vendor and staff behavior may adapt, model inputs may drift, applicants may alter applications and documentation, and pool boundaries or political budgets may change. Transport therefore requires:

- evidence across multiple saturation levels for nonlinear dose-response and capacity congestion;
- validation in populations, cycles, regions, need levels, and institutional conditions that cover the target policy;
- explicit models or bounds for award displacement, review demand, applicant behavior, staffing, vendor pricing, learning, drift, and cross-pool effects;
- audit of overlap between experimental and statewide populations and transparent reweighting only where support exists;
- at least one new-cohort, held-out replication and stress tests at near-universal saturation while preserving the floor; and
- a clearly stated invariance assumption linking experimental potential outcomes and equilibrium rules to full adoption, with sensitivity analysis and renewal gates.

If the state is effectively one capacity pool or universal adoption changes the allocation rule itself, the full-policy effect may require a phased cluster rollout across independent cohorts, structural or partial-identification analysis, and post-adoption monitoring; it cannot be inferred by scaling the individual ITT.

## Empirical statuses and conditional recommendations

| Claim | Status | Regulatory interpretation |
|---|---|---|
| 13% administrative-cost reduction | **Provisional descriptive report** | Unaudited definition, baseline, shifted costs, and uncertainty; not yet a real-resource saving. |
| Five-day lower mean decision time for high-score applicants | **Provisional descriptive report** | Unaudited and selected by score; no causal effect or systemwide queue gain established. |
| Increased vendor profit | **Provisional accounting report** | Incidence and decomposition unknown; neither welfare gain nor harm by itself. |
| Total awards unchanged | **Provisional aggregate accounting report** | Expected under a fixed cap; says nothing about winner identity, displacement, or welfare. |
| 26% would seek no-delay review | **Provisional stated-preference fact** | Signals potentially material suppressed demand; subject to survey sampling, wording, and hypothetical bias. |
| Score improves accuracy, access, enrollment, debt, or subgroup outcomes | **Underidentified / unmeasured** | Required inputs, errors, withdrawals, downstream outcomes, and subgroup performance are unaudited. |
| Current pilot is a Pareto improvement | **Not established / unsupported** | No joint no-loser welfare evidence; displacement and review burdens are unresolved. |
| Universal-adoption effect | **Underidentified and not transportable from current evidence** | Fixed-cap interference, equilibrium adaptation, and saturation response are unknown. |

**Conditional normative recommendation:** under a rights-constrained, distribution-sensitive welfare criterion, pause consequential score use now; provide independent no-delay review and notice to current applicants; preserve and audit records; disclose the contract and allocation rule to the regulator; and do not scale until the protection floor is operational and distributional net benefits are demonstrated. A regulator using a pure cost-benefit criterion would still lack the resource, incidence, displacement, and outcome evidence needed to approve adoption.

**Conditional research recommendation:** continue only if the floor is verified, genuine uncertainty remains about relative merits, pools and review capacity make partial interference credible, and the two-stage design is preregistered with independent governance. Treat floor breach, missing audit access, material cross-pool interference, inadequate cluster count, or inability to measure displaced applicants as stopping conditions. If those conditions cannot be met, abandon experimental consequential use and evaluate the score offline against audited historical or prospectively collected outcomes without affecting awards.
