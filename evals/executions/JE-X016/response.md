The strongest research question is:

> How does a congestion charge reshape commuting, firm and household location, transit crowding, and land rents in spatial equilibrium—and what are its efficiency and distributional consequences once congestion, crowding, relocation, fiscal revenue, and transition costs are jointly counted?

Reduced car trips are an intermediate outcome. They may reflect efficient substitution, but also suppressed valuable trips, displacement to unpriced roads or times, costly relocation, or overcrowded transit.

### Competing mechanisms

1. **Pigouvian correction**
   - Drivers internalize delay, pollution, crash risk, and road scarcity.
   - Traffic falls, remaining trips become faster, and aggregate surplus may rise.

2. **Mode substitution with transit crowding**
   - Commuters switch to transit.
   - Welfare depends on service expansion, reliability, crowding, and whether fares cover marginal operating costs. A road externality can partly become a transit externality.

3. **Trip suppression and unequal constraint**
   - Some travelers cannot flex hours, work remotely, or afford the charge.
   - Fewer trips may mean foregone work, care, education, or social activity rather than efficient substitution.

4. **Firm relocation and agglomeration**
   - Firms may leave the charged zone to reduce employee and delivery costs, or enter because roads become faster and central accessibility improves.
   - Relocation can weaken agglomeration economies or reallocate activity toward more productive locations.

5. **Housing-demand capitalization**
   - Improved accessibility may raise housing demand and rents inside or near the zone; avoidance incentives may raise demand outside it.
   - Owner gains can coexist with renter displacement. Observed rent changes combine accessibility, amenity, expectations, and composition effects.

6. **Traffic and pollution displacement**
   - Trips may shift to boundary roads, exempt vehicles, other times, or neighboring jurisdictions.
   - Central improvements can therefore coexist with external costs elsewhere.

7. **Revenue-recycling mechanism**
   - Welfare depends on who pays and how revenue is used: transit capacity, lump-sum rebates, tax reductions, or general expenditure.
   - Treat revenue as a transfer unless its collection or use changes real resources; do not count it mechanically as a net social benefit.

### Welfare object

Start with an explicit social surplus measure such as

\[
\Delta W =
\Delta CS_{\text{travel/housing}}
+\Delta \Pi_{\text{firms, transit, landlords}}
+\Delta R_{\text{government}}
-\Delta C_{\text{operations}}
-\Delta E_{\text{congestion, pollution, crashes}}
+\Delta A_{\text{agglomeration}}
-\Delta C_{\text{moving/adjustment}}.
\]

Avoid double counting: land-value changes often capitalize accessibility and amenity benefits already represented in household utility. Separate:

- efficiency from incidence;
- short-run effects, with fixed residences and firm sites, from long-run spatial equilibrium;
- owners from renters;
- flexible professionals from shift workers and caregivers;
- central residents from boundary neighborhoods;
- current residents from movers and entrants.

Report both aggregate equivalent variation and its distribution. If interpersonal welfare weights matter, show results under several transparent weighting schemes. Also report non-monetized outcomes—access, displacement risk, disability constraints, and environmental inequality—rather than silently assigning them zero value.

### Empirical-first path

Use this if the main contribution is credible causal evidence within the observed policy environment.

1. Establish policy timing, boundaries, exemptions, anticipation, enforcement, contemporaneous transit changes, and revenue use.
2. Measure outcomes at appropriate spatial and temporal resolution: traffic speeds and volumes, trip destinations, transit loads and reliability, employment and establishments, leases and vacancies, rents and transactions, pollution, and household moves.
3. Exploit designs such as boundary discontinuities, event studies with credible comparison cities or corridors, differential exposure by pre-policy commuting routes, and staggered implementation if available.
4. Explicitly test spillovers and displacement; nearby untreated areas are not valid controls if traffic, firms, or households relocate into them.
5. Use mechanism-discriminating evidence:
   - faster roads plus stable total accessibility supports congestion correction;
   - peak crowding and reliability deterioration indicate transit-capacity costs;
   - firm moves concentrated among delivery-intensive businesses support logistics costs;
   - rent changes tracking accessibility rather than simple boundary status support capitalization;
   - traffic increases on boundary roads reveal displacement.

This path can identify local causal effects and incidence. It generally cannot, by itself, recover full welfare or predict a different charge, boundary, transit investment, or revenue-recycling rule.

### Structural path

Use a structural spatial-equilibrium model if the policy target requires unobserved welfare, long-run relocation, or counterfactual policy design.

Include:

- heterogeneous households choosing residence, workplace, mode, route, and possibly departure time;
- firms choosing location, employment, and delivery intensity;
- endogenous road congestion and transit crowding;
- housing supply and rents;
- agglomeration or productivity spillovers;
- government revenue and transit capacity;
- moving, job-switching, and firm-relocation costs.

Identify travel preferences from mode, route, or time variation; location preferences and moving costs from migration and rent responses; firm-location elasticities from establishment transitions; housing supply from construction constraints; and congestion/crowding technologies from engineering and operational data. Validate against untargeted outcomes, especially boundary traffic, transit crowding, rent gradients, and firm entry/exit.

A productive hybrid is often best: use reduced-form estimates to establish policy effects and discipline key elasticities, then use the structural model only for equilibrium welfare and out-of-sample counterfactuals. Compare at least:

- no charge;
- alternative charge levels and boundaries;
- peak versus flat pricing;
- exemptions or targeted rebates;
- revenue-funded transit expansion;
- coordinated pricing across neighboring jurisdictions.

The decisive next step is to build an institutional and data map. If individual travel, transit loads, rents, firm locations, and policy implementation can be linked spatially over time, pursue the hybrid design. If only aggregate traffic counts exist, narrow the claim to congestion and displacement effects rather than promising full welfare.
