# Regulatory assessment

## Decision

The mayor’s Pareto claim is **not established**. A Pareto improvement requires credible evidence that no affected person is worse off. The pilot instead documents selected agency and vendor outcomes while leaving applicant losses—false denial, delayed or lost housing, waiting-list exit, family separation, homelessness duration, and unequal errors—unmeasured. A citywide mandate should therefore **pause**. Continue only a time-limited, independently governed evaluation above a binding protection floor; suspend the score if that floor cannot be implemented or if sentinel harms breach preset limits.

## Economic and institutional diagnosis

Applicants seek housing under scarcity, urgent need, limited outside options, and unequal ability to navigate digital and administrative processes. They can submit information, remain or exit, accept offers, and challenge decisions, but cannot inspect the records or logic materially shaping their priority. A nominal review choice that adds about four weeks is not an equivalent alternative when housing delay is itself harmful.

Caseworkers choose how much to rely on the score and how to allocate attention. Managers seek throughput, placements, budget control, and political credit. The vendor seeks profit, renewal, and protection of proprietary assets. The city controls housing capacity, procurement, data access, the decision rule, and appeal procedure; the procurement office’s role as adjudicator creates at least an appearance of institutional conflict. Families and service providers bear spillovers, while taxpayers receive any real resource savings.

The score may improve targeting by combining records, standardize inconsistent judgment, or shift effort toward labeled high-risk applicants. Rival mechanisms fit the same figures: relabeling or prioritization can redistribute a fixed stock of offers; staff may defer to the score; difficult cases may be displaced, delayed, or induced to exit; and the vendor may optimize measured throughput while harms remain off the dashboard. Because offers are allocated from shared scarce capacity, interference is constitutive rather than a nuisance: one applicant's priority can change others' placement chances and waiting times. A 7% placement increase for one labeled group can therefore reflect direct targeting, displacement within an offer pool, spillovers to other pools or periods, increased utilization, or some combination. The relevant capacity pools—such as property, bedroom size, eligibility class, geography, and allocation period—must be defined from actual offer-substitution rules.

Information and power are asymmetric. Applicants cannot verify inputs, denial reasons are coarse, and the city/vendor can define both the score and success metrics. Errors may reflect prior service contact rather than underlying need, reproducing unequal surveillance or administrative visibility. Mandating the system would also change behavior: applicants may alter service use or reporting, caseworkers may deskill or game thresholds, agencies may change recordkeeping, and the vendor may gain lock-in and bargaining power. Pilot effects therefore need not transport to citywide equilibrium.

## What the reported evidence does—and does not—show

- The reported 14% cost decline and six-day fall in mean processing time are **provisional accounting/descriptive results** until the baseline, denominator, comparison group, time window, case mix, uncertainty, implementation costs, appeal costs, and costs shifted to applicants or other agencies are audited. Means can conceal a harmful upper tail.
- The 7% increase in placements among those *labeled* high risk is not necessarily a 7-percentage-point change, a causal effect, an increase among truly high-need applicants, or a net increase in placements. Label composition may have changed, and gains may be reallocation from unlabeled applicants.
- Higher vendor profit is producer surplus and distributional incidence, not a separate social resource saving. Payments are transfers; only net changes in real resource use count once in a social account. The account should decompose normal return to vendor inputs and risk, economic rents from market power or proprietary lock-in, and costs shifted to applicants, workers, or other agencies.
- The 31% survey response is stated demand for review under a hypothetical zero-delay condition. It indicates potentially substantial latent concern, not actual take-up, welfare loss, error prevalence, or the proportion who would prevail. Wording, sample selection, information, hypothetical bias, and fear of disclosure matter. The gap from observed review use cannot be interpreted as satisfaction because the current four-week penalty selects who can afford to appeal.

No single welfare account has yet been produced. A defensible account must combine real administrative resources, housing and homelessness outcomes, risk and delay, family effects, applicant time and legal costs, fiscal incidence, vendor rents, distribution across protected and vulnerable groups, and non-tradable commitments to due process, dignity, privacy, and equal treatment. Whether aggregate gains could compensate losers (Kaldor–Hicks), whether compensation is actually paid, and whether some rights may not be traded are separate questions. Evidence cannot choose the distributional weights or acceptable rights constraints.

## Nonrandomizable protection floor

Every applicant, in every arm, must receive:

1. no adverse decision based solely on the score; an accountable trained official must make and sign the decision;
2. timely notice of score use, the material data fields and sources, a specific intelligible reason, and a free way to inspect and correct records;
3. independent appeal outside procurement, with counsel/advocate access where needed, accessible language and disability accommodations, and **no loss of queue position or added processing delay** from review;
4. a human reconsideration service available through digital and non-digital channels, with nonretaliation and documented override authority;
5. data minimization, security, retention limits, audit access, vendor disclosure sufficient for validation, and a ban on secondary use;
6. continuous subgroup and intersectional monitoring, including false adverse decisions and downstream harms, plus preset stop rules and rapid remedy (restored priority, expedited placement, and compensation where warranted).

This floor is not an experimental arm. If scarce capacity makes delay-free review impossible, capacity must be funded before further experimentation; scarcity does not justify charging due process to the applicant.

## Evaluation above the floor

First conduct a retrospective data-and-process audit: reconstruct score inputs and versions; validate records against applicant corrections; define the true decision pathways; report score calibration, ranking performance, false-adverse rates, overrides, appeals, exits, and outcome distributions by disability, migration status, race, family structure, digital access, and prior service contact, including intersections. Link to placements, time housed, homelessness duration, family separation, and repeat applications. An independent evaluator and affected-applicant advisory body should preregister outcomes, subgroup thresholds, uncertainty, stopping rules, and publication rights.

If equipoise remains, first define capacity pools using actual substitutability of offers across applicants and time. Prefer **pool-level assignment** between (A) floor-compliant usual prioritization and (B) the same floor plus score availability, or randomize prespecified score-availability **saturation** across comparable pools and periods. An exposure mapping should record each applicant's own assignment, the treated share and composition of competitors in the relevant pool, pool capacity, and treatment in connected pools or adjacent periods. Pool boundaries and exposure mappings must be fixed before outcomes are examined. Applicant-level randomization is acceptable only if the design deliberately varies saturation and can identify the resulting interference; ordinary individual randomization with a shared fixed stock cannot support a no-interference analysis. Randomize and conduct design-based inference at the actual assignment level, stratifying on capacity, office, time, and prespecified vulnerability composition.

**Receipt** is actual score exposure and consequential use in a case, separately logged from assignment. Outcomes include timely valid placement, days unhoused, false adverse decision, processing-time distribution, review/override, exit, family separation, applicant burden, agency resource cost, and subgroup gaps. Prespecify: (i) direct effects of own assignment at fixed pool exposure; (ii) displacement effects on applicants competing for the same offers; (iii) spillovers across connected pools and later periods; and (iv) the total-policy effect on all eligible applicants, placements, waiting time, and costs at each randomized saturation. Report effects on both labeled and unlabeled applicants; “labeled high risk” is post-assignment if the label is unavailable in control and is generally not a valid conditioning variable. Any applicant ITT is specific to the design's capacity, pool definition, exposure mapping, and saturation; it is not the effect of a universal citywide mandate. Extrapolation to full saturation requires either randomized support near full saturation or an explicitly validated allocation model, with uncertainty and equilibrium assumptions reported.

Noncompliance includes assigned teams not consulting the score, consulting it without relying on it, control contamination, applicant withdrawal, and missing follow-up. Preserve assignment-based analysis; do not treat actual users as comparable to nonusers. If pool assignment is used as an instrument for consequential score use, a complier average causal effect requires random assignment, relevance, exclusion (assignment affects outcomes only through defined receipt), monotonicity (no one is induced away from receipt), well-defined treatment, no differential attrition, and an interference structure fully captured by the prespecified exposure mapping. Exclusion is especially doubtful because merely offering a score can change attention, documentation, delay, and competitors' allocations even without logged reliance; absent defensible exclusion and exposure assumptions, report assignment effects by saturation and do not claim a complier effect.

Mandate decisions should require replicated net benefits, no floor breach, acceptable subgroup harm bounds rather than merely nonsignificant disparities, transparent total-cost accounting, and evidence that capacity and vendor governance remain credible at scale.

## Branch decision

**Pause the citywide mandate; continue only the safeguarded audit and, if the floor and equipoise hold, a preregistered independent evaluation.** Backtrack to institutional redesign or abandon the score if transparent correction, delay-free independent review, valid harm measurement, or enforceable vendor access cannot be secured. Consider expansion only after the evaluation supports applicant-centered net benefit and the city explicitly resolves the remaining distributional and rights choices through legitimate public process.
