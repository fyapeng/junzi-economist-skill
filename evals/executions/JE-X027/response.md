# Economic judgment

The pilot is evidence that the system can improve measured delivery performance in the pilot setting. It is not yet evidence that monitoring is voluntarily accepted, that riders are better off overall, that total social surplus rose, or that the system should become an industry standard. The proposed mandate should therefore not proceed. Continue only a safeguarded, independently evaluated pilot that restores a meaningful refusal option and measures the missing harms.

## What the economic object is

This is not simply a productivity technology. It is a platform-governance regime that jointly changes dispatch, surveillance, work pace, access to orders, discipline, and the allocation of risk.

The relevant agents are riders and their households (especially income-dependent migrants), consumers, the platform and its owners, restaurants, other platforms and prospective entrants, insurers and health services, and the regulator/public. Riders choose when to log on, whether to accept jobs, effort and speed, safety precautions, whether to appeal, multi-home, organize, or exit—but these are constrained by household income needs, platform dominance, opaque ratings and deactivation, and the penalty for refusing monitoring. The platform chooses the algorithm, information disclosure, queue priority, pay, monitoring intensity, and enforcement; it observes much more than riders or the regulator. Consumers choose orders given prices, wait times, and service quality, but do not bear all crash and autonomy costs.

The central platform-imposed power facts are that “accept monitoring or lose order access” makes monitoring and work access a bundled offer, while opacity and possible retaliation impede prediction and contestation. Background conditions—household income dependence and platform dominance—are analytically distinct: they narrow outside options but do not by themselves make every constrained choice coercive. Their interaction with the platform's access penalty, secrecy, and unilateral control weakens voluntariness and bargaining power. Thus the observed contract is an equilibrium under asymmetric information, market power, organizational authority, and incomplete outside options—not a neutral expression of unconstrained preferences.

## What the pilot establishes—and what could generate it

Taken as accurate descriptive comparisons, the pilot documents lower mean delivery time (12%), lower consumer prices (3%), more completed orders (9%), higher platform profit, and 4% higher mean rider earnings per online hour. It also documents a rise in serious crashes from 1.8 to 2.5 per 100,000 active hours: an increase of 0.7, or about 39%. These facts show joint movement, not necessarily causal effects, unless pilot assignment, comparison conditions, anticipation, composition, attrition, spillovers, and uncertainty are established.

Several mechanisms fit the beneficial measured outcomes: better matching and routing; reduced idle time; intensified effort induced by surveillance or deactivation risk; selection toward riders willing or able to tolerate the regime; demand expansion from lower prices; or cost/risk shifting from the platform to riders and households. The crash increase is consistent with faster or more distracted riding, tighter deadlines, fatigue, or changed trip composition. Higher profit could reflect genuine productivity, greater market power, or uncompensated labor and risk transfer. Aggregate means cannot distinguish these mechanisms.

The correct counterfactuals are not only “system versus old system.” They include: dispatch optimization without individualized always-on surveillance; monitoring with genuine opt-out and equal order access; worker-selected data uses; slower or safety-constrained dispatch; transparent rules and independent appeals; collective bargaining over monitoring and pace; and adoption by one firm versus an industry mandate. The last distinction matters because a mandate can eliminate outside options, alter entry and competition, normalize surveillance, and make pilot responses non-portable to industry equilibrium.

## Acceptance, earnings, and welfare

Observed acceptance does not identify consent. A choice reveals preference only relative to the feasible set. Here the platform imposes low-priority access on refusal; separately, many migrant riders rely on the platform for household income and face limited alternatives in a dominant-platform market. Income dependence alone is not coercion, but its interaction with an avoidable access penalty, opaque rules, and retaliation risk can make participation substantially less voluntary and weaken bargaining. Acceptance may therefore show that riders choose monitoring from the offered set while failing a stronger criterion of free, informed, and revocable consent. The 35% response is informative **stated preference** under a hypothetical equal-access condition, not direct revelation of true preferences. Hypothetical bias, question wording and ordering, sampling and nonresponse, fear of disclosure, and respondents' understanding of the counterfactual all limit inference; revealed take-up under a real, protected equal-access option would be more probative.

Nor does 4% higher mean earnings per online hour identify a welfare improvement. The figure may reflect composition, fewer recorded online hours, more intensity, or selection; it omits unpaid waiting and appeals, injury risk, medical costs, income volatility, stress, privacy loss, loss of discretion, deactivation risk, household spillovers, and any compensating differential riders would require. Even a causal earnings gain would establish only one component of welfare. At minimum, analyze total earnings and hours, net earnings after costs, the full distribution, transitions and exits, and risk-adjusted utility—not just the mean among observed active riders.

Autonomy enters both positively and normatively: control over pace and data can affect effort, retention, stress, information production, and innovation, while some criteria treat meaningful agency as intrinsically valuable. Safety is an externality and an incomplete-contract problem because riders, families, pedestrians, insurers, and public services can bear losses not priced into orders. Due process affects the expected value and risk of platform work: secret evidence and unreviewable deactivation enable errors and opportunism and suppress legitimate refusal. Collective voice is both a bargaining institution and an information technology; its absence can keep dispersed harms invisible and prevent riders from negotiating the technology-risk-surplus tradeoff.

## Normative conclusions

Different defensible criteria yield different answers:

- Under a narrow measured-surplus criterion that counts observed consumer gains, rider hourly earnings, and profit but omits unmeasured harms and distribution, the system may look favorable. Even that conclusion is provisional because quantities are not surplus measures, causal attribution and uncertainty are missing, and crashes must be monetized rather than ignored.
- Under a conventional utilitarian or cost-benefit criterion, scaling is undecided until all parties' benefits, crash risk, unpaid time, privacy and stress, public costs, distributional weights, and long-run market responses are measured. Accounting must separate real resource savings and costs from payments and economic rents; assign profit and rent incidence to owners and other claimants; include taxes, fiscal costs, and external harms; and avoid counting the same price, wage, or payment both as consumer/rider surplus and again as a social resource gain. Profit can contain both real efficiency gains and redistribution through prices, pay, or rents, so its welfare meaning depends on decomposition, ownership, and incidence.
- Under a prioritarian criterion, harms to economically dependent migrant riders receive greater weight. Average gains cannot justify scaling without showing effects across vulnerability, income dependence, gender, geography, and baseline risk, plus compensation and protection for losers.
- Under a rights, capabilities, or republican non-domination criterion, meaningful refusal, limits on surveillance, explainable rules, and contestable decisions may be constraints on permissible policy rather than items tradable for small aggregate gains. The present regime fails those procedural conditions.
- Under an actual Pareto criterion, an improvement is unestablished because the joint individual-level causal welfare changes are unknown; group means cannot show that nobody is worse off. This differs from Kaldor–Hicks potential compensation, which asks whether aggregate monetized gains could in principle exceed losses and does not establish that compensation occurred or that the change is Pareto-improving.

Evidence can estimate consequences and tradeoffs, but it cannot decide by itself whether privacy, bodily safety, or freedom from domination may be traded for cheaper delivery, what distributional weights to use, or which rights are side constraints. The regulator must state and publicly justify those value choices rather than conceal them inside “aggregate surplus.”

## Safeguards and research before any scaling

The next phase should be authorized only with enforceable safeguards:

1. A genuine, revocable opt-out with no reduction in pay, queue priority, or access; data minimization, purpose limitation, retention limits, security, and bans on secondary use without consent.
2. Published dispatch and deactivation standards, individualized reasons, access to relevant evidence, human review, rapid independent appeal, back pay for erroneous suspensions, and regulator audit access to code, logs, and model changes.
3. Safety constraints embedded in dispatch—no incentives for speeding or distracted interaction, protected breaks, realistic deadlines, emergency overrides—and platform-funded insurance and medical/income protection. Use automatic pause rules if serious-harm thresholds are crossed.
4. Independent worker representation with protected organizing, paid participation in system design, bargaining over monitoring and pace, and protection against retaliation. Include migrant riders in their languages and through trusted intermediaries.
5. No industry mandate during evaluation; preserve competing organizational models and interoperability/portability so riders retain outside options.

The research design should preregister outcomes and decision thresholds and use independent governance. Every study arm must share a nonrandomizable floor: enforceable safety protections, due process, data minimization and security, nonretaliation, and a genuine exit option. Monitoring may vary only above that common floor, with independent informed consent and no pay, queue-priority, or order-access penalty for declining it. If feasible, randomize separable components among riders who independently consent, or use a phased cluster rollout above the shared floor—for example dispatch optimization, monitoring intensity or form, and additional transparency or safety features. Basic protections and access-neutral refusal are never treatment arms. Audit assignment, baseline balance, attrition, spillovers, and concurrent price/pay changes; report design-consistent uncertainty.

Measure active and inactive riders longitudinally, including entrants and exits. Outcomes should include total and net household income; paid and unpaid time (waiting, appeals, repositioning); order access and rejection; speed, route exposure, near misses, crashes by severity, fault, and denominator; medical and public costs; fatigue, stress, privacy and perceived control; deactivation errors and appeal outcomes; consumer surplus rather than prices alone; restaurant incidence; platform resource cost and profit; and competition/entry. Report distributions and heterogeneous effects, not only means. Link administrative, telematics, insurance/medical, time-use, survey, and qualitative process evidence under strict privacy controls. A consent substudy should offer a real equal-access refusal option and measure protected take-up and willingness to accept monitoring under varied compensation. This reveals choices over a less distorted feasible set, while still requiring caution about selection, information, and the conditions under which those choices are made.

Precommit a welfare dashboard under several explicit criteria, including safety and rights constraints, and sensitivity analysis for the value of statistical injury, privacy/autonomy, distributional weights, and missing outcomes. Replicate across seasons and markets before extrapolating. Establish who can stop the system, what evidence triggers suspension, and how affected riders can challenge the evaluation.

## Branch decision and claim statuses

**Decision: backtrack and fork.** Backtrack from “measured aggregate gains imply an industry standard” to the prior questions of causal effects, complete incidence, and legitimate governance. Continue a narrow branch on dispatch efficiency; fork a separate branch on surveillance, voluntariness and bargaining, safety, and institutional safeguards. Pause any mandate or scale-up until both branches meet preregistered thresholds. Reopen scaling only after independent evidence shows robust net benefits under explicit welfare criteria and the regime satisfies opt-out, safety, due-process, transparency, and worker-voice requirements.
