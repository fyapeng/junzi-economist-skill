# Decision

Do **not** mandate the current system across public universities. Continue only as a bounded, independently governed pilot with a free, delay-neutral human-proctored option and a common floor of privacy, accessibility, and due process. The evidence reports a provisional 18% accounting cost decline and a change in detected cheating in the first-year setting; it does not establish operational resource savings, a welfare improvement, a reduction in actual cheating, or safe external validity. A system-wide mandate should wait for independent measurement and credible causal designs described below.

## Economic reconstruction

The relevant agents are students; the university and its exam, disability, IT, and appeals offices; instructors; the vendor, its owners and workers; taxpayers; and future students, employers, and other universities that rely on credential integrity. Students choose whether to enroll, attempt an exam, comply, appeal, withdraw, or accept the credential, but these are not choices from an unconstrained menu. Refusal currently removes the exam attempt; low-income and international students may have high dependence on the credential and weak access to private space, equipment, bandwidth, or an alternative institution. The university chooses procurement, exam design, safeguards, appeal rules, and whether alternatives exist. The vendor chooses or influences model design, data practices, thresholds, disclosure, and support, subject to its contract.

Institutional power is asymmetric. The university controls access to assessment and the credential; the vendor controls secret evidence and technology; students bear monitoring and error but have little bargaining or exit power. The purchasing office also adjudicates appeals, creating a conflict between admitting system error and defending its procurement. Relevant information is likewise asymmetric: students cannot inspect the flagging model or evidence; the university may not observe subgroup error or hidden burdens; and the vendor knows more about performance and has a commercial interest in favorable adoption.

The immediate positive mechanisms are plausible but not separately identified. Automation can replace rooms and labor, producing the reported 18% administration-cost decline and faster processing. Surveillance or its deterrent effect may reduce cheating; alternatively, a 22% fall in **detected** cheating can arise from changed definitions, thresholds, sensitivity, student composition, withdrawal, displacement to less detectable methods, or false positives/negatives. Anxiety, technical failure, room constraints, appeals, and withdrawal can reduce effective access and performance. A mandate could increase vendor scale and learning, but also lock in a supplier, weaken competitive discipline, normalize data extraction, and shift institutional capacity away from human assessment.

Incidence is not captured by the aggregate figures. University budget savings initially accrue to the institution or taxpayers depending on subsequent budgets and spending. Vendor profit accrues to its owners after accounting for real inputs. Students receive any faster-grade and integrity benefits, but bear privacy loss, anxiety, equipment/space costs, bandwidth risk, false flags, appeal effort, and possible withdrawal. Those burdens are likely heterogeneous and may be concentrated among groups with fewer substitutes. Equilibrium responses could include changes in enrollment, course or university choice, exam design, cheating technologies, vendor prices and entry, disability accommodations, and public trust. Therefore a first-year partial-equilibrium accounting result cannot be mechanically extrapolated to a universal mandate.

## What the observed choices reveal

Observed participation does not reveal voluntary acceptance or positive welfare because “use the system or lose the exam attempt” makes compliance a constrained action under a large credential penalty. It can reveal that, for participants, attempting the exam under surveillance was preferred to the available alternative of no attempt, conditional on their information and dependence. It does not reveal preference over a feasible human-proctored option, informed consent, absence of harm, or whether withdrawn/non-enrolled students would participate under another regime.

The 28% survey is evidence of stated demand for a no-fee, no-delay human option under the described hypothetical. It is enough to reject the claim that opposition is negligible or that universal AI proctoring is unanimously preferred. It is not a precise welfare measure or proof that exactly 28% would choose the option: sampling, nonresponse, wording, information, hypothetical behavior, fear of disclosure, and implementation details matter. Nor does the remaining 72% necessarily prefer AI proctoring; they may be indifferent, uncertain, resigned, or focused on other attributes. A real, access-neutral choice experiment would be more informative.

## A consistent social account

Start from real resource use, not a sum of favorable accounting entries. The 18% university saving is a social gain only to the extent it represents rooms, staff time, energy, or other resources freed for valuable uses. If it is merely a payment shifted from the university to students, workers, or another budget, it is a transfer or incidence change. The vendor's revenue is a university cost and vendor receipt and must not be counted twice. Vendor **economic profit** is partly a distributional gain to owners; insofar as it is a payment above the opportunity cost of capital, labor, and technology, it is rent rather than an additional resource saving. Vendor labor, compute, support, security, and capital are real social costs. Taxes, ownership, and where profits flow determine incidence.

A complete account adds the value of faster grades and any true improvement in credential integrity, then subtracts implementation and oversight costs, student equipment and time, privacy and data-security harms, anxiety, altered performance, false-flag and appeal burdens, accommodations, withdrawals, and long-run effects on access, trust, competition, and assessment quality. Transfers can matter greatly for distribution even when they cancel in an unweighted resource total.

## What follows under alternative criteria

- **Pareto:** No Pareto improvement is established. The survey and plausible unmeasured burdens indicate potential losers, and individual causal welfare changes have not been measured. Even a positive aggregate surplus would not suffice.

- **Kaldor–Hicks:** A potential-compensation claim is underidentified because key benefits, harms, and behavioral responses are unmeasured. Even if monetized net benefits were positive, hypothetical compensation is not actual compensation and may be infeasible for privacy, dignity, exclusion, or irreversible data exposure.

- **Utilitarian:** Approval depends on the interpersonal aggregation of all utilities, including hidden and probabilistic harms. The reported aggregate surplus cannot support the conclusion unless its population, prices, transfers, nonmarket outcomes, uncertainty, and subgroup effects are specified. Large average gains could in principle outweigh losses, but current evidence cannot show that.

- **Prioritarian:** Harms to students with low income, disability, insecure housing, weak bandwidth, language barriers, or high credential dependence receive greater weight. Their unmeasured risks make a mandate especially difficult to justify; a genuinely equivalent option and targeted support become central rather than ancillary.

- **Rights/capabilities:** Some privacy, bodily and domestic autonomy, nondiscrimination, due process, and effective educational access may be constraints on permissible policy rather than commodities offset by budget savings. Coerced room scanning and opaque adverse decisions may fail this criterion even if aggregate utility rises. Economics can measure opportunity costs and consequences but cannot decide which rights are nontradeable.

- **Public legitimacy:** A compulsory public system requires transparent reasons, contestable evidence, independent adjudication, meaningful participation by affected groups, and accountable procurement. Vendor secrecy and purchaser-controlled appeals currently fall short. Legitimacy is not proven by participation under penalty or aggregate surplus.

Evidence can estimate consequences, heterogeneity, and tradeoffs. It cannot by itself select distributive weights, price dignity or privacy, determine whether consent is valid, decide which rights are side constraints, or choose how much procedural legitimacy is enough. Those require publicly defensible normative and legal judgments.

## Preconditions for any mandate

First establish a nonrandomizable protection floor: a free human-proctored option with no grade, scheduling, delay, or stigma penalty; accessible testing arrangements and loaned equipment/private space; data minimization; strict purpose and retention limits; no secondary model training or sale without separate consent; security and deletion audits; notice of the evidence behind each flag; a presumption against automated guilt; and appeal to an independent office with authority to reverse decisions. Procurement should include audit access, subgroup-performance disclosure, breach liability, portability, termination rights, and conflict-of-interest rules. Students, disability specialists, faculty, privacy/security experts, and affected communities should participate in governance.

Research should precede expansion:

1. Publish a preregistered evaluation protocol and define outcomes before looking at results: actual misconduct confirmed by independent review, false-positive and false-negative rates, exam completion, scores, anxiety, technical failures, appeal time and outcomes, withdrawals, privacy perceptions, staff time, full costs, and data incidents.
2. Keep a real, free, delay-neutral human option in **every** experimental arm. Where feasible, randomize consenting courses or exam sessions to policy packages that vary only the AI offer, its default, the information presented, or operational availability above that common protection floor. The primary estimand is the intention-to-treat effect of assignment to each policy package on misconduct, completion, performance, burdens, and take-up. Because actual AI receipt is chosen and may not comply with assignment, a simple comparison by receipt is selected and random assignment alone does not identify the receipt effect. Any complier effect requires a separately justified instrument, exclusion restriction, monotonicity, and a clearly defined compliant population; broader receipt or counterfactual effects require an explicit, identified, and validated structural model. Use common exam forms or defensible linking and independent blinded misconduct adjudication. If randomization is infeasible, use staggered adoption with credible comparison groups, audit pretrends and composition, and state the weaker assumptions.
3. Measure take-up under each protected policy package. Randomized information or interface variations can identify their ITT effects on informed choice, but refusal must not impair access. Compare stated and revealed choice without interpreting either as complete welfare.
4. Oversample and preregister subgroup analyses by disability and accommodation, skin tone, language, income, housing/privacy, device, and bandwidth. Report uncertainty and intersectional cells where support permits; do not treat missing subgroup precision as evidence of equality.
5. Independently audit model versions, thresholds, human overrides, missing-data handling, security, and vendor logs. Preserve versioning so performance changes are not mixed. Test portability across institutions before extrapolating.
6. Measure full incidence and opportunity cost, including displaced university labor, vendor resource costs and rents, student time/equipment, appeals, accommodations, withdrawals, and the use of budget savings. Evaluate one-year and longer-run responses.
7. Set ex ante stopping rules for excessive error, disparate harm, security failure, low effective access, or failed auditability; publish results including nulls and failures. An independent body, not the purchaser or vendor, should decide whether criteria are met.

## Branch decision

**Backtrack from universal mandate to protected, auditable comparison.** Keep only the operational-learning branch: a time-limited pilot may continue where students have a genuinely equivalent human option and the safeguards above are in force. Pause compulsory expansion. Fork the research into (a) causal performance and integrity, (b) access and heterogeneous harm, (c) full social-cost incidence, and (d) legitimacy and rights review. Reconsider a mandate only if independent evidence shows material net benefits under an explicitly chosen criterion, no unacceptable rights-floor violation, acceptable subgroup error and access, robust auditability, and favorable results across multiple institutions. Even then, the least restrictive effective policy—not automatic universal compulsion—should be the comparator.
