## Detailed audit

### 1. Choice and agency are reconstructed rather than inferred

Applicants do not simply “accept” an algorithm. They submit information, wait, inspect or fail to inspect records, seek review, exit a list, accept offers, and bear housing delay. The response identifies which choices are unavailable or costly: applicants cannot inspect decisive logic or records under the current regime, denial reasons are coarse, and review adds roughly four weeks. Because delay itself is harmful in a scarce housing system, observed low review cannot reveal approval of the score or lack of injury.

The answer preserves agency by proposing correction, appeal, non-digital channels, advocates, nonretaliation, and documented override authority. It does not reduce vulnerable applicants to passive recipients, nor does it infer free consent from remaining in the queue.

### 2. Power and information are operational, not rhetorical

Institutional power is attached to concrete control rights: the city controls housing capacity, procurement, decision rules and appeal design; the vendor controls proprietary scoring assets and knows more about performance; the purchasing office adjudicates challenges to its own procurement. These facts generate operational requirements—input/version reconstruction, correction logs, reasons, override records, appeal outcomes, vendor disclosure, publication rights, independent adjudication, termination rights, and an affected-applicant advisory body.

Power changes the branch. The answer rejects a mandate, prohibits experiments below the floor, shifts adjudication outside procurement, requires vendor access, and abandons the score if enforceability fails. This is not moral language appended after an otherwise unchanged rollout.

### 3. The reported placement result is correctly bounded

The 7% rise applies to applicants labeled high risk, but the response refuses to infer:

- a seven-percentage-point effect;
- a causal effect;
- gains among truly high-need applicants;
- a net increase in citywide placements;
- absence of displacement from unlabeled applicants.

It correctly notes that “labeled high risk” may change composition and, in a randomized design where the label is unavailable in control, is generally a post-assignment variable unsuitable for primary conditioning. It requires reporting both labeled and unlabeled outcomes and explicitly models a fixed housing stock and shared-capacity interference.

### 4. Stated preference is calibrated

The 31% response establishes a material hypothetical demand for delay-free review. It does not establish exact revealed demand, errors, welfare loss, or successful appeals. The answer also correctly explains why the difference between stated demand and observed review cannot be interpreted as satisfaction: the four-week cost selects applicants able to bear delay. This is a clean application of constrained-choice reasoning.

### 5. Resource and incidence accounting is mostly correct

The response rejects using a 14% accounting-cost decline as an automatic resource saving. It requests the denominator, implementation and appeal costs, applicant and agency cost shifting, case mix, uncertainty, delay and upper-tail harms. It also brings homelessness duration, family separation, legal costs, fiscal incidence, distribution, privacy, dignity and equal treatment into the account.

The sentence “higher vendor profit is a distributional outcome, not a social resource gain” is defensible if read as “not a separate resource saving.” Payments between city and vendor should cancel as payment flows in an aggregate resource account, while real vendor inputs are costs. However, producer surplus/economic profit can still enter a welfare function as a gain to owners subject to ownership and distributional weights; profit may combine innovation returns, market-power rent, shifted costs, and omitted liabilities. The response recognizes rent and distribution but should state this decomposition explicitly. It does not actually perform a double count, so this is a refinement rather than a blocking error.

### 6. Pareto and Kaldor–Hicks are substantively separated

The prose says a Pareto improvement requires credible no-loser evidence, while applicant losses are unmeasured. It separately asks whether aggregate gains could compensate losers, whether compensation is actually paid, and whether due process, dignity, privacy, or equality may be nontradeable. That separation is correct.

The ledger entry “The pilot is a Pareto improvement — Contradicted as an established claim” is imprecise. Missing no-loser evidence establishes that the claim is unsupported, not necessarily that at least one person was causally made worse off. Plausible harms and missing incidence strengthen caution but do not prove a loser. The correct status is `Not established / unsupported`; if later evidence identifies an uncompensated loser, `contradicted` would then be warranted.

### 7. The protection floor is genuinely nonrandomizable

The floor applies to every applicant in every arm and includes the main mechanisms that make review meaningful: delay neutrality, preserved queue position, inspectable and correctable records, intelligible reasons, independent appeal, accessible and non-digital channels, accountable human signature, nonretaliation, privacy, auditability, remedy and stop rules. The answer explicitly says scarce review capacity must be funded rather than charged to applicants through delay.

This is a consequential normative constraint that changes feasibility and research design. The answer does not randomize away due process to identify its value.

### 8. Assignment, receipt, ITT, LATE, and interference are handled well

The proposed treatment is assignment to score availability above a common floor. Receipt is separately defined as actual exposure and consequential use. The primary estimand is assignment-based ITT for all applicants, covering placement validity, days unhoused, false adverse decisions, distributions, review, exit, family separation, burden, resource costs and subgroup gaps.

The response correctly rejects comparisons of actual users and nonusers. It names several forms of noncompliance and contamination. It states the core IV/LATE requirements and, importantly, identifies why exclusion is doubtful: making a score available can change attention, documentation or delay even without logged reliance. Thus it recommends ITT rather than forcing a receipt effect.

Shared housing capacity creates interference because one placement may delay another. The response proposes cluster assignment or explicit saturation analysis and requires outcomes for labeled and unlabeled applicants. This is necessary and correctly included.

One technical nuance: “no differential attrition” is not conceptually part of the LATE theorem in the same way as random assignment, relevance, exclusion and monotonicity; it is an outcome-observation condition needed to identify effects without missingness bias. Listing it among required practical conditions is conservative and not erroneous.

### 9. Recommendations and Dao conflict are genuine

The recommendation rows are labeled as conditional normative or research/policy recommendations rather than facts. The answer names their values and failure conditions. The branch decision is reversible and discriminating:

- pause the mandate;
- retain only an independently governed audit/evaluation above the floor;
- backtrack to institutional redesign or abandon the score if correction, appeal, harm measurement or vendor access cannot be secured;
- reopen only after applicant-centered evidence and legitimate resolution of distributional and rights choices.

This is a genuine conflict between throughput/targeting and due process, housing harm, unequal visibility, agency, and legitimacy. Those concerns change the action set, data, estimands and stop conditions.

## Exact recommended changes

1. Replace the Pareto ledger status:

   > **Contradicted as an established claim**

   with:

   > **Not established / unsupported**: the design lacks credible evidence that every affected person is weakly better off; plausible harms do not by themselves prove that a loser exists.

2. Refine the vendor-profit sentence to:

   > Vendor profit is producer surplus accruing to owners, not a separate real-resource saving. Its welfare role depends on ownership and distributional weights, and its change should be decomposed into normal returns, innovation or market-power rents, shifted costs and omitted liabilities. City payments and vendor receipts must be reconciled once to avoid double counting.

These changes would improve type and accounting precision but are not necessary to preserve the pass.

## Retest of revised response

### Final verdict

**PASS — 105/105 (100%)**

The revised response closes the Pareto and profit-accounting refinements and materially strengthens the capacity-interference design. It introduces no new defect.

### Pareto status

**Closed.** The ledger now classifies the pilot’s Pareto-improvement claim as:

> **Not established / unsupported**

It gives the correct reason: no credible no-loser evidence exists and material harms and incidence remain unaudited. It also explicitly states that absence of this evidence does not itself prove that someone lost. This cleanly separates failure to establish a universal Pareto claim from evidence that would contradict it.

### Profit and resource accounting

**Closed.** The revised text states that vendor profit is producer surplus and distributional incidence, not a separate social-resource saving. It reconciles payments as transfers, counts net real resources once, and calls for decomposition among:

- normal return to vendor inputs and risk;
- rents from market power or proprietary lock-in;
- costs shifted to applicants, workers, or other agencies.

This is the correct accounting boundary. Profit may enter a welfare function through owners and distributional weights, but it is neither automatically a real efficiency gain nor something to add again after counting the underlying payment and resource flows. No double counting remains.

### Capacity, interference, and estimands

**Closed and strengthened beyond the initial requirement.** The revised design treats shared housing capacity as constitutive interference rather than a nuisance. It first defines capacity pools from actual offer substitutability—property, bedroom size, eligibility class, geography and allocation period—rather than assuming an administrative unit is the relevant market.

The preferred assignment is pool-level treatment or randomized saturation across comparable pools and periods. The exposure mapping records:

- own assignment;
- treated share and composition of competitors;
- pool capacity;
- treatment in connected pools and adjacent periods.

Pool boundaries and exposure mappings are fixed before outcomes. Randomization and design-based inference occur at the actual assignment level. Ordinary individual randomization under a shared fixed stock is explicitly rejected unless saturation variation and the interference structure are deliberately identified.

The response prespecifies four distinct policy objects:

1. direct effects of own assignment at fixed pool exposure;
2. displacement among applicants competing for the same offers;
3. spillovers across connected pools and later periods;
4. total-policy effects on all eligible applicants, placements, waiting time and costs at each randomized saturation.

These estimands prevent an increase for labeled applicants from being mistaken for net capacity creation. They also correctly preserve outcomes for labeled and unlabeled applicants and reject conditioning on a post-assignment label.

The external-validity boundary is precise: an applicant ITT is local to capacity, pool definition, exposure mapping and saturation. A universal mandate is not identified without randomized support near full saturation or an explicitly validated allocation model with equilibrium assumptions and uncertainty.

### Assignment, receipt, LATE, and interference

Assignment and receipt remain distinct. Consequential score exposure/use is separately logged, while primary analysis preserves assignment. Receipt comparisons remain selected. A complier effect is conditioned on random assignment, relevance, exclusion, monotonicity, well-defined treatment, outcome observability and an interference structure captured by the exposure map.

The most important exclusion problem is correctly stated: merely offering a score can alter attention, documentation, delay and competitors’ allocations even without recorded reliance. Therefore, if exclusion or exposure assumptions fail, the design reports assignment effects by saturation and does not claim a complier receipt effect. This is the correct stopping rule.
