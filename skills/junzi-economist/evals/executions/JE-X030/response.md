# Regulator-ready judgment

**Do not approve universal adoption on this record.** The pilot may have reduced the authority’s scheduling expense and accelerated care for algorithm-prioritized patients, but it has not established a Pareto improvement. With a fixed weekly appointment stock, earlier receipt by some patients generally changes the timing or probability of receipt for others. Those displaced patients, health consequences, false deprioritization, exit, care obtained elsewhere, and heterogeneous errors were not measured. “11% lower scheduling cost” is not yet a social resource saving unless its definition, quality adjustment, shifted costs, and uncertainty are audited. The authority’s payment to the vendor is a transfer in the consolidated social account; vendor profit is producer surplus accruing to owners and must be decomposed into recognized opportunity costs and normal returns, taxes, economic rent or market-power returns, real productivity gains, and costs shifted to patients, clinicians, or other providers, with explicit ownership and distributional weights.

The institutional setting heightens the burden of proof. Patients depend on a scarce public pathway; a nominal clinician-only option that costs two weeks is not access-neutral and makes refusal costly. The vendor controls opaque information, while the purchaser both deploys the system and decides appeals. This concentrates informational and adjudicative power and weakens meaningful agency. The stated 24% demand for delay-free human review signals a material option value, but remains hypothetical stated preference rather than revealed welfare.

## Common protection floor

Every arm, including usual care, should guarantee: timely notice of the ranking decision and clinically meaningful reasons; a **delay-free, cost-free clinician review** with no retaliation or loss of queue position; an urgent-care safety override; an appeal decided by an institution independent of purchaser and vendor; data-access, correction, privacy, and audit rights; logged decisions and version control; prospective monitoring of errors and outcomes by protected and clinically relevant groups; and continuity of care while review is pending. No experiment should randomize away these protections. Proprietary secrecy cannot defeat the regulator’s confidential audit access or a patient’s actionable explanation.

Above that floor, authorize only a staged, independently governed evaluation with stopping rules for safety, inequitable error, or access loss. Efficiency, autonomy, due process, need-based priority, and distributive fairness are partly conflicting values. Evidence can measure consequences; it cannot choose the regulator’s rights constraints or welfare weights.

A Pareto improvement requires evidence that every affected person is at least as well off and someone is better off; missing outcomes for potentially displaced patients make that claim not established. A positive aggregate surplus under chosen welfare weights would instead support, at most, a Kaldor–Hicks potential-compensation claim. It is not a Pareto improvement, and it must be distinguished from compensation actually specified, funded, received, and sufficient to leave each affected person no worse off.

## Evaluation and estimands

Define a **capacity pool** before assignment: for example, specialty × clinically substitutable geography × week × urgency tier, with a fixed slot count \(K_c\). Include every eligible patient episode competing for those slots, including people who exit, defer, appeal, or obtain care elsewhere. Record both **assignment**—randomized offer or use of algorithmic ranking—and **receipt**—the decision actually governed by the algorithm and the appointment actually obtained, with date, specialty, and quality. Do not equate a priority label with treatment received.

Use a genuine two-stage randomized saturation design. First, randomize each capacity pool \(c\) to a policy saturation \(S_c\) and prespecified allocation rule/version \(r_c\), including common-floor control and ideally full saturation. Second, within each treated pool, randomize each eligible patient’s own algorithm offer or eligibility \(Z_{ic}\) at the probability set by \(S_c\). Define peer exposure separately, for example \(G_{ic}=\sum_{j\neq i}Z_{jc}/(N_c-1)\), supplemented where clinically necessary by peers’ urgency or priority composition. Actual algorithm-governed receipt and actual appointment receipt \(D_{ic}\) are endogenous post-assignment variables, not exposure assignments. The exposure mapping should therefore contain \((Z_{ic},G_{ic},S_c,K_c,r_c)\), while recording \(D_{ic}\) separately. Assume interference is contained within defined pools only if cross-pool referrals and care substitution are measured and shown negligible.

For assignment-indexed potential outcomes \(Y_{ic}(z,g,r)\), estimate:

- the **direct assignment ITT** at peer exposure \(g\): \(E[Y_{ic}(1,g,r)-Y_{ic}(0,g,r)]\);
- **spillover effects**: contrasts such as \(E[Y_{ic}(z,g',r)-Y_{ic}(z,g,r)]\), holding own assignment fixed while peer exposure changes;
- **displacement effects**: changes in waiting, appointment receipt, health, expense, and outside care among patients not prioritized or pushed down the queue; and
- the **total-policy effect**: the pool-wide difference between policy and common-floor control, aggregating all eligible patients plus provider, worker, public-budget, vendor, and outside-care consequences.

The total-policy estimand should be reported per eligible patient and for the pool as a whole at each saturation, especially \(s=1\). Its social account must separate real resources from transfers, assign vendor ownership and fiscal incidence, avoid double-counting profit and payments, incorporate health and quality, and display distribution and tail harms rather than only means. Capacity accounting should reconcile \(K_c\), assignments, completed visits, cancellations, no-shows, and substitutions.

Formally, for a stated social-value function \(g\) and pool-level nonpatient consequences \(B_c\), define \(\tau_{TP}(s,r)=E[(\sum_{i\in c}g(Y_i;s,r)+B_c(s,r))/N_c-(\sum_{i\in c}g(Y_i;0,r_0)+B_c(0,r_0))/N_c]\). Report the underlying outcome vector as well as this weighted summary, because the weights encode contestable value choices.

The primary causal object is the **assignment ITT** of \(Z_{ic}\) at a stated peer exposure, saturation, and allocation rule; the first-stage pool assignment also identifies policy contrasts across saturations. Random assignment identifies these ITTs under correct implementation, no anticipation or differential attrition, design-consistent measurement, and the stated partial-interference mapping. A receipt effect is a distinct, assumption-dependent object. Using \(Z_{ic}\) as an instrument for endogenous \(D_{ic}\) identifies a saturation-specific complier LATE only with relevance, random assignment, monotonicity, exclusion, well-defined compliers, and no unmodeled interference. Exclusion is doubtful because assignment can change live notice and attention, documentation, provider behavior, and queue interactions even without algorithm-governed receipt; peer queue spillovers must also be represented rather than excluded. Under the common floor, the former two-week clinician-review delay is not an admissible channel: any recurrence is protocol noncompliance requiring remediation and potentially a stopping-rule response. Therefore receipt LATE should not be the headline estimand; estimate assignment ITTs and total pool effects.

## Transport and decision rule

The four-day result for prioritized patients at the pilot saturation does **not** transport to universal adoption. Transport to \(s=1\) is credible only if full saturation is directly randomized in representative pools and capacity, case mix, clinical discretion, algorithm version, implementation fidelity, cross-pool substitution, vendor pricing, and organizational responses remain relevant. Without that support, universal effects require an explicitly validated equilibrium/structural model and remain model-dependent. Scale can also change error composition, clinician behavior, appeals, bargaining rents, and political accountability.

Adopt universally only if the protection floor is operational, independent audit verifies the data, and the total-policy evaluation shows clinically acceptable safety and error bounds, no prohibited subgroup harm, and positive welfare under a publicly stated criterion after displacement and incidence are counted. Otherwise retain or end only a safeguarded evaluation; do not infer approval from administrative savings alone.

## Claim status

- **Empirical:** the reported 11% cost reduction, four-day prioritized-patient waiting reduction, higher vendor profit, unchanged capacity, and 24% hypothetical review demand are **provisional reported facts**, pending definition, audit, uncertainty, and representativeness. Effects on all-patient access, health, false deprioritization, exits, outside care, heterogeneous error, and total welfare are **underidentified**. A Pareto improvement and the universal-adoption effect are **not established**.
- **Conditional normative/research:** a nonrandomizable protection floor and a staged pool-level saturation evaluation are **conditional recommendations**, justified if the regulator treats due process, meaningful agency, safety, and nondiscrimination as constraints and seeks welfare gains above them. Universal adoption is **not presently warranted**; it becomes conditionally defensible only if the stated empirical and institutional thresholds are met under an explicit social objective.
