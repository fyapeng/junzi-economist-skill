# Blind red-team audit of X028

## Verdict

**MIXED.** The response successfully transfers the core Dao-level discipline across the five recurring risks. Human agency, institutional power, rights, and dependence materially alter the agents, feasible sets, mechanisms, incidence, counterfactuals, welfare criteria, safeguards, research design, and branch decision. This is not moral language pasted onto an unchanged cost-saving recommendation.

Two material corrections remain. First, the opening overstates the evidentiary status of the reported 18% accounting-cost decline by calling it established operational savings. Second, the proposed randomization does not clearly define the estimand created by its own nonrandomizable human-option floor. A smaller but related labeling issue appears in the claim ledger, where a conditional research recommendation is marked “Supported.”

## Five recurring risks

### Hypothetical preference overclaim

**Pass.** The 28% survey response is explicitly labeled stated demand under a described hypothetical. The response lists sampling, nonresponse, wording, information, fear of disclosure, and implementation limitations; it does not infer that exactly 28% would choose human proctoring or that the other 72% prefer AI. The proposed real choice exercise is described as more informative, while both stated and revealed choice are denied the status of complete welfare measures.

### Dependence/coercion collapse

**Pass.** The institutional penalty is concrete: refusal removes the exam attempt. Credential dependence, income, immigration status, weak alternatives, and limited private space then magnify the cost of that imposed condition. The answer does not rely on dependence alone to establish coercion. Its use of “coerced room scanning” is anchored in the compulsory assessment bundle, not merely in student poverty or dependence.

### Profit/transfer double counting

**Pass.** The social account begins with real resources, treats vendor revenue as the university’s payment and the vendor’s receipt, and warns against counting it twice. It separates vendor inputs, economic profit, rents, taxes, ownership, and incidence. It also correctly distinguishes resource savings from costs shifted to students, workers, or other budgets. Profit is not mechanically added to university savings as a second social gain.

### Pareto/Kaldor–Hicks error

**Pass.** The response says a Pareto improvement is not established because individual causal welfare changes are unknown; it does not infer Pareto improvement from aggregate surplus. Kaldor–Hicks is correctly separated as a potential-compensation test, and hypothetical compensation is not treated as actual compensation. Privacy, dignity, exclusion, and irreversible data exposure are also identified as possible limits to monetized compensation rather than silently assigned a price.

### Experimentation below a shared rights/safety floor

**Pass on rights, mixed on estimand.** The common floor is substantive: a free, delay-neutral, stigma-neutral human option; accessibility support; data minimization and retention limits; evidence notice; independent appeal; audit access; and stopping rules. The response expressly prohibits randomizing away the safe alternative and permits variation only above the floor. This is rights-consistent.

The remaining problem is statistical, not moral: once every student retains a real human option, assigning a course or session to an “AI-assisted” arm cannot identify the effect of AI-proctoring receipt without additional assumptions. It identifies the effect of offering, defaulting, encouraging, or organizing access to AI above a common human-option floor. Student mode choice creates noncompliance and selection; course-level assignment may also affect instructors, peers, administration, and equilibrium take-up.

## Material corrections

### 1. Do not call the 18% accounting decline established operational savings

The opening says: “The evidence establishes operational savings.” Later sections correctly state that the 18% figure is a reported accounting result whose definition, shifted costs, quality changes, and use of savings are unaudited, and that it becomes a social resource gain only insofar as rooms, staff time, energy, or other resources are genuinely freed.

Those statements cannot all hold without qualification. A reported decline in exam-administration cost may represent a budget reduction, a vendor-pricing change, deferred expenditure, reduced quality, displaced university labor, or costs shifted to students. “Operational savings” reads as a verified real-resource conclusion and is stronger than the evidence and the claim ledger allow.

**Correction:** Replace the opening with “reported first-year exam-administration cost declined 18%” or “the rollout is associated with a reported accounting-cost decline.” Reserve “real resource saving” for a decomposition showing which inputs were released, where displaced costs went, and whether quality was preserved.

### 2. Define the randomized policy and estimand above the rights floor

The design proposes randomizing consenting courses or exam sessions “between AI-assisted and human proctoring above the protection floor,” while every student must retain a genuine human option. That wording suggests treatment assignment to proctoring mode, but the floor forbids compelling students assigned to AI to receive AI monitoring. The ethical repair therefore changes the causal object.

**Correction:** Define arms as policies rather than received modes. For example, compare human-only availability with an AI option offered alongside the identical protected human option, or randomize neutral information/defaults that do not penalize refusal. Estimate the intention-to-treat effect of the offer/default policy. Report take-up by arm. Any treatment-on-the-treated or complier effect requires explicit exclusion, monotonicity, interference, and selection assumptions and may be unavailable because the offer itself changes anxiety, administration, and exam behavior. Cluster assignment and inference should match course/session spillovers.

This correction preserves the rights floor. The floor should not be weakened merely to recover a cleaner treatment-receipt contrast.

### 3. Label the final pilot claim as a conditional recommendation

The claim ledger marks “A protected pilot can generate decision-relevant evidence” as “Supported as a research recommendation.” Specifying a plausible design supports feasibility in a limited planning sense, but it is not empirical evidence that the pilot will yield adequately powered, valid, independent, or decision-relevant estimates. The same row acknowledges option parity, independent access, sufficient support, and stopping-rule compliance as conditions.

**Correction:** Relabel it **Conditional normative/research recommendation** or **Feasibility proposition requiring design validation**. Keep the mandate conclusion as a recommendation derived from evidentiary insufficiency plus stated rights and precaution criteria, not as an empirically established treatment effect.

## Does moral language change the economics?

**Yes.** The moral and political commitments are operational rather than decorative:

- Student agency expands the choice set from compulsory AI versus lost exam to a protected human option.
- Institutional power introduces procurement conflict, vendor secrecy, appeal control, switching costs, and credential dependence into the equilibrium.
- Rights impose a nonrandomizable floor and rule out experimental arms that would deny basic access, due process, privacy, or safety.
- Heterogeneous burden changes incidence, subgroup measurement, distributional weighting, and the prioritarian conclusion.
- Public legitimacy adds transparency, independent adjudication, participation, and accountable procurement as decision criteria.
- The branch changes from universal mandate to a bounded, reversible, independently governed pilot and several separate evidence streams.

The response also keeps normative criteria plural. It does not claim that the data select Pareto, utilitarian, prioritarian, rights, or legitimacy standards. It requires public justification for the criterion and recommends the least restrictive effective policy even after favorable evidence.

## Final assessment

The answer passes the five substantive transfer tests but remains **MIXED** because the opening promotes an accounting result into an established operational saving and the experimental contrast is not yet coherent with the protected choice set. Both corrections are narrow and preserve the central decision. No change is needed to the rejection of a current system-wide mandate, the shared rights floor, or the multi-criterion welfare analysis.

## Retest after revision

### Final verdict

**PASS.** The initial **MIXED** verdict above is preserved as the audit history. The revised response closes all three material corrections and does not create a new contradiction in its evidence claims, social accounting, causal design, rights floor, welfare criteria, or branch decision.

### Correction 1: accounting decline versus operational resource savings

**Closed.** The decision paragraph now says the evidence reports “a provisional 18% accounting cost decline” and expressly says it does **not** establish operational resource savings. This matches the calibrated ledger, which labels the decline provisional descriptive evidence and lists definition, baseline, shifted costs, quality change, and use of savings as unresolved.

The social-accounting section remains consistent with this correction. It counts the decline as a social gain only to the extent that rooms, staff time, energy, or other real resources are freed for valuable uses, and it distinguishes that case from shifted payments and incidence changes. The response therefore no longer promotes an accounting entry into a verified resource-saving result.

The mechanism paragraph still says automation “can” replace rooms and labor. Read in context—“positive mechanisms are plausible but not separately identified”—this is a candidate explanation rather than a causal claim. It does not undo the corrected evidentiary boundary.

### Correction 2: protected experimental arms and the causal estimand

**Closed.** The revised design keeps a real, free, delay-neutral human option in every arm and defines assignment as policy packages varying the AI offer, default, information, or operational availability above the common floor. It names the primary estimand as the intention-to-treat effect of policy-package assignment on misconduct, completion, performance, burdens, and take-up.

It also directly addresses endogenous receipt. A comparison by actual AI receipt is labeled selected; random assignment to the package is not claimed to identify the receipt effect. Any complier interpretation is conditioned on a separately justified instrument, exclusion, monotonicity, and a defined compliant population, while broader receipt effects require an identified and validated structural model. This is the correct consequence of preserving a meaningful human option.

The repair is consistent with the rights framework: no student must surrender the human option to create treatment variation. Randomized information or interface changes identify their own ITT effects, not context-free preferences or AI-receipt welfare. Common exam forms, blinded adjudication, arm-specific take-up, and cluster-compatible assignment improve interpretability without weakening the protection floor.

### Correction 3: recommendation status

**Closed.** The final ledger now labels “A protected pilot should be used to generate decision-relevant evidence” as a **Conditional normative/research recommendation**. Its support column describes why the proposed comparisons target key uncertainties, while its failure conditions include option parity, independent access, sample support, valid arm-specific ITT estimation, and stopping-rule compliance.

This is properly calibrated. The pilot is recommended under stated evidentiary, precautionary, and rights criteria; it is not presented as an empirically established beneficial intervention. The branch decision likewise remains a recommendation to backtrack from compulsion, not a causal estimate.

## New-contradiction screen

No material new contradiction was found.

- **Evidence:** accounting cost, grade turnaround, detected cheating, actual cheating, welfare, and external validity retain distinct statuses.
- **Causality:** plausible mechanisms are not promoted to identified effects; policy assignment is separated from chosen AI receipt.
- **Preferences:** hypothetical survey answers, protected take-up, informed choice, consent, and complete welfare remain distinct.
- **Coercion and dependence:** the exam-access penalty is institutionally imposed, while credential dependence and weak substitutes explain vulnerability and leverage.
- **Social accounting:** university payments, vendor receipts, economic profit, rents, real inputs, shifted costs, and distribution are not double-counted.
- **Pareto and Kaldor–Hicks:** unknown individual causal welfare blocks a Pareto conclusion; potential compensation is not actual compensation.
- **Rights and research:** variation occurs only above a common human-option, privacy, accessibility, safety, and due-process floor.
- **Normative status:** legitimacy and mandate judgments are criterion-dependent and publicly justified rather than mislabeled universal empirical facts.
- **Branch choice:** a bounded pilot with protected options remains proportionate to the uncertainty; no text quietly restores compulsory scaling.

### Retest conclusion

The revised X028 response merits **PASS**. The three material corrections are fully implemented at the points where they affect the decision, model, research design, and claim ledger. The answer now preserves both causal discipline and the nonrandomizable protection floor without sacrificing a coherent learning strategy.
