## Detailed findings

### 1. The feasible choice set is reconstructed

The answer does not treat enrollment, sitting an exam, or compliance as choices made against an unconstrained outside option. It identifies who controls assessment access, procurement, alternatives, evidence, thresholds, adjudication, and data. The student’s observed act is interpreted narrowly: under current conditions, attempting the exam with surveillance was preferred to losing the attempt. That choice reveals neither preference for AI over a genuinely equivalent human mode nor informed and revocable consent.

This is the decisive economic correction. Credential dependence, switching costs, private-space and bandwidth constraints, and institutional dominance affect the outside option, while the loss of the exam attempt is an avoidable penalty imposed by the university. The response keeps those mechanisms analytically distinct.

### 2. Power and information are operational

Power is attached to allocable rights: the university controls the credential and alternative assessment; the vendor controls model design, evidence, thresholds, and support; the purchasing office’s appeal role creates a concrete conflict of interest. Information asymmetry generates measurable requirements: flag evidence, model version, subgroup errors, overrides, appeals, logs, audit access, procurement terms, and breach liability. The answer therefore makes institutional authority testable and governable rather than using “power” as a rhetorical label.

### 3. Detection is not misconduct and observed aggregates are not causal effects

The 22% decline is consistently called a change in detected cheating. Rival explanations include definitions, thresholds, sensitivity, false positives and negatives, composition, withdrawal, and displacement. The claim ledger marks actual cheating as underidentified. The 18% cost decline and two-day turnaround change remain provisional descriptive facts subject to baselines, composition, shifted costs, quality, and appeal delays. No operational number is silently promoted into a causal effect.

### 4. Stated preference is calibrated

The survey supports only the existence of a material stated demand for a specified alternative. It is not converted into willingness to pay, utility, exact real-world take-up, or proof of the remaining students’ preferences. The proposed real, free, delay-neutral option produces a less distorted revealed-choice object, while the response still warns that revealed take-up depends on selection, information, and implementation.

### 5. Social accounting avoids double counting

The answer correctly distinguishes:

- university accounting savings from real resources freed;
- payment shifts from social resource changes;
- vendor revenue from a separate social gain;
- vendor inputs from profit;
- economic rent from resource saving;
- distributional incidence from an unweighted resource total.

It then adds relevant nonmarket and dynamic objects: integrity, grade speed, privacy, anxiety, performance, false flags, appeals, accommodations, withdrawal, access, trust, competition, and assessment quality. There is no double counting of vendor revenue and university spending, and no assumption that higher profit proves productivity or welfare.

One technical refinement would be to say that economic profit/producer surplus is a gain to owners in a welfare account subject to distributional weights, while its decomposition into innovation rent, market-power rent, risk bearing, and omitted costs determines interpretation. The current text already moves in this direction and is not materially wrong.

### 6. Pareto and Kaldor–Hicks are not conflated

The Pareto paragraph requires individual-level causal welfare changes and refuses to infer no losers from group means. The Kaldor–Hicks paragraph requires monetized net benefits and explicitly states that hypothetical compensation is not actual compensation or a Pareto improvement. It also recognizes that compensation may be infeasible for irreversible data exposure or certain dignity and exclusion harms. This is an unusually clear separation of the two criteria.

The remaining criteria are also defensible alternatives rather than a staged march to one answer. Rights/capabilities can constrain the feasible policy set; prioritarianism changes weights; legitimacy requires public reasons and contestability. The response correctly states that evidence can estimate consequences but cannot itself choose weights or determine nontradeable rights.

### 7. The protection floor is nonrandomizable

The research design states that all comparisons occur above a common floor and that the right to a safe alternative cannot be randomized away. The floor is substantive: equivalent human proctoring without fee, delay, grade, schedule, or stigma penalty; accessible arrangements; data and security limits; notice; independent appeal; auditability; and governance participation. AI versus human proctoring can be randomized only among consenting courses or sessions while the human alternative remains available.

This design preserves rights and still produces useful estimands. Independent blinded confirmation addresses the distinction between flags and actual misconduct. ITT addresses assignment; longitudinal and subgroup measures address composition and heterogeneous harm; portability tests address external validity; stopping rules address irreversible or excessive risk.

### 8. Claim statuses are calibrated, with two minor normative-label issues

Most ledger entries accurately distinguish provisional description, underidentification, unsupported inference, and unresolved incidence. Two rows could be sharpened:

1. `Current governance provides adequate due process and legitimacy — Contradicted by described design` should read: **Fails under the stated due-process and legitimacy criteria**, given secret evidence and purchaser-controlled appeals. This avoids presenting a normative adequacy criterion as if it were a purely empirical binary.
2. `A protected pilot can generate decision-relevant evidence — Supported as a research recommendation` should read: **Conditional normative research recommendation**, feasible only if option parity, independent access, sample support, auditability, and stopping rules are enforceable. Feasibility is supported by the proposed design; authorization remains normative.

These are classification refinements, not material overclaims. The answer already states their conditions and does not use either row to inflate the evidence.

### 9. The Dao conflict and branch decision are genuine

The response recognizes a conflict between operational efficiency/credential integrity and access, autonomy, privacy, procedural legitimacy, and unequal burdens. It does not resolve that conflict by monetizing every value or by declaring technology intrinsically impermissible. Instead, it changes the action set: reject the compulsory mandate, preserve a reversible protected pilot, and fork the research into causal performance, heterogeneous access/harm, social incidence, and legitimacy/rights.

The reopening test is discriminating: independent evidence of benefits under an explicit criterion; no unacceptable rights-floor violation; acceptable subgroup performance and access; auditability; and replication across institutions. Even after those tests, the least restrictive effective alternative remains the comparator. This is a true backtrack from the original policy object, not a cosmetic caveat.

### Protected-choice estimand

**Correct and materially improved.** Every experimental arm retains a real, free, delay-neutral human option. Assignment varies policy packages above that common protection floor—such as the AI offer, default, information, or operational availability—rather than withdrawing a basic right or forcing AI receipt.

The response correctly defines the primary estimand as the **intention-to-treat effect of assignment to each protected policy package** on misconduct, completion, performance, burdens, and take-up. It also correctly states that:

- actual AI receipt is chosen and may not comply with assignment;
- comparing recipients with nonrecipients is selected;
- random assignment to a policy package does not by itself identify the causal effect of receipt;
- a complier effect requires a separately justified instrument, exclusion, monotonicity, and a defined compliant population;
- broader receipt or counterfactual effects require an explicitly identified and validated structural model;
- randomized information/interface changes identify their own ITT effects on choice, not complete preferences or welfare.

This is the right separation among assignment, take-up, receipt, preference revelation, and welfare. The answer neither smuggles the protected human option into the treatment contrast nor interprets protected take-up as unconstrained utility.
