# Scarce Telemedicine Appointments, Access, and Displacement: Evidence from a Randomized Invitation

## Abstract

Can scarce telemedicine appointments improve access for patients facing severe travel barriers without merely reallocating care across patients? We study 2,400 preregistered rural adults who had no household car, lived more than 30 kilometers from specialty care, and had a clinician-documented need for follow-up. During the first three months of a regional platform, invitations to book limited appointments were randomized within clinician and week blocks. An invitation increased completion of clinically indicated follow-up within 30 days by 0.150 from a control mean of 0.44 (95% CI [0.101, 0.199]) and reduced round-trip travel time by 90 minutes from a control mean of 138 minutes (95% CI [-113.5, -66.5]). Twenty-five percent of invitees did not complete a platform visit. Although the corresponding Wald ratio is 0.20, it is not a credible treatment-receipt effect without exclusion, monotonicity, and no interference; invitation messages could affect scheduling directly, and patients shared clinicians. Observational saturation comparisons suggest possible displacement of noninvited patients, while the clinician-week estimate of total completed follow-up is too imprecise to establish net access expansion. Estimates for health, spending, and clinic exit are also imprecise and do not demonstrate equivalence. The experiment therefore shows that appointment invitations improved invitees’ access and reduced travel, but it does not establish health gains, savings, systemwide access expansion, or positive net social welfare.

## Introduction

Remote care can relax one constraint while tightening another. For patients who live far from specialty services and lack reliable transportation, a virtual visit can sharply reduce the time required to obtain clinically indicated follow-up. Yet telemedicine still uses scarce clinician time, scheduling capacity, and platform resources. When those inputs are fixed in the short run, giving one patient priority may displace another rather than expand total care. This distinction matters for both positive and normative evaluation. An intervention may substantially improve access for the people offered an appointment even when its effect on aggregate access is small, uncertain, or negative. Evidence on invitees alone therefore answers an important question, but not the full question faced by a health system allocating scarce appointments.

This paper studies that distinction during the first three months of a regional telemedicine platform in rural counties. The study population consists of 2,400 preregistered adults with unusually high barriers to in-person specialty care: every participant had no household car, lived more than 30 kilometers from specialty care, and had a clinician-documented need for follow-up. Limited invitations to book telemedicine appointments were randomized at the individual level within clinician and week blocks. Allocation logs and baseline balance were independently checked. The intervention was an invitation, not platform entry and not completed treatment: 25 percent of invitees did not complete a platform visit. The experiment thus identifies the intention-to-treat effect of being invited among preregistered high-barrier patients under the implemented allocation rule.

The central question is whether an invitation changes two immediate margins of access: completion of clinically indicated follow-up and the travel burden associated with that follow-up. The design is well suited to this question because randomization occurred among patients facing the same local implementation period and within clinician-week blocks. The relevant counterfactual is assignment without an invitation, not absence of the regional platform. Consequently, the estimates do not identify the effect of introducing telemedicine at the county level, and they need not generalize to residents outside the preregistered population. Those boundaries are substantive. Preregistration selected adults with documented clinical need and severe transportation constraints, precisely the population for whom remote appointments might have especially large private benefits.

The invitation produced a large increase in timely follow-up. Within 30 days, the probability of completing clinically indicated follow-up rose by 0.150, with a randomization-inference standard error of 0.025 and a 95 percent confidence interval of [0.101, 0.199]. The control mean was 0.44, so the estimate represents a sizable change relative to follow-up under assignment without an invitation. This result establishes improved access for invited patients on the prespecified completion margin. It does not by itself reveal whether the increase came entirely from completed telemedicine encounters. An invitation could also provide information, confer scheduling priority, or induce patients to arrange in-person care.

Travel burdens fell even more sharply. The invitation reduced round-trip travel time for follow-up by 90 minutes, relative to a control mean of 138 minutes. The randomization-inference standard error is 12 minutes and the 95 percent confidence interval is [-113.5, -66.5]. This outcome assigns zero travel to telemedicine follow-up and therefore captures substitution toward remote care as well as changes in whether follow-up occurred. Travel time is missing for 4 percent of the treatment group and 5 percent of the control group. Inverse-probability adjustments and worst-case bounds still preserve a reduction of at least 61 minutes. The travel result is therefore not driven by the observed difference in missingness. It directly documents a reduction in a costly access burden, although the study does not convert the saved time into money or a welfare measure.

The experiment provides less information about downstream outcomes. At 90 days, the estimated effect on a standardized health index is 0.010 standard deviations (SE 0.030; 95% CI [-0.049, 0.069]). The estimated effect on total medical spending is $20 (SE $45; 95% CI [-$68, $108]), and the estimate for clinic exit is -0.003 (SE 0.006; 95% CI [-0.015, 0.009]). None of these estimates establishes a detectable change under the study’s precision, but none is an equivalence test. The health interval, for example, is compatible with modest gains and modest losses. The spending interval permits either savings or increases. These results should therefore be read as imprecise evidence rather than as proof that telemedicine leaves health, expenditures, or retention unchanged.

Treatment take-up creates a further interpretive boundary. Invitations increased completed platform visits by 0.75. Dividing the follow-up intention-to-treat estimate by this first stage gives a Wald ratio of 0.150/0.75 = 0.20. That arithmetic does not, on its own, identify the causal effect of receiving a telemedicine visit for compliers. Such an interpretation would require exclusion, monotonicity, and no interference. Exclusion is not assured because an invitation message may change information or scheduling behavior even when the recipient never completes a platform visit. No interference is also questionable because invited and noninvited patients draw on shared clinicians. We therefore treat 0.20 as a descriptive Wald ratio, not as an established local average treatment effect.

Shared capacity also changes how the individual estimates enter a system-level assessment. Other preregistered patients assigned to the same clinician but not invited had follow-up fall by 0.050 (SE 0.020) relative to comparable clinician-weeks with lower invitation saturation. At the clinician-week level, total completed indicated follow-up rose by 0.020 per eligible patient (SE 0.018; 95% CI [-0.015, 0.055]). These patterns raise the possibility that some gains among invitees came at the expense of noninvited patients. They do not prove displacement, however, because invitation saturation was not randomized. Clinician-weeks with higher saturation may have differed in demand, staffing, or other determinants of follow-up. Likewise, the aggregate interval includes both a small reduction and a meaningful increase, so the study does not establish whether the platform expanded total completed care.

This combination of randomized assignment and nonrandom saturation yields a deliberately asymmetric conclusion. The individual invitation estimates retain a clear causal interpretation for assigned patients under the implemented randomization: invitation improved their completion of indicated follow-up and reduced their travel time. The spillover comparisons are observational and should be treated as evidence of a possible capacity channel, not as identified causal effects. Once shared clinicians are acknowledged, a simple no-interference interpretation is unavailable for welfare analysis. The direct intention-to-treat effect remains informative, but summing only invitees’ gains would omit potentially displaced care.

The evidence also leaves the mechanism only partially resolved. Reduced travel is directly measured and is consistent with substitution away from in-person follow-up. Improved information and scheduling priority remain plausible because the intervention was an invitation and not merely receipt of a video encounter. The data contain no direct measure of clinical quality. A completed follow-up could represent improved continuity and convenience, but completion alone cannot show that the clinical content was equally or more effective than the relevant in-person alternative. The near-zero health-index estimate does not resolve this issue because its confidence interval is not narrow enough to establish equivalence and the horizon is only 90 days.

An exploratory subgroup result should be separated from these prespecified findings. After viewing the results, the researchers selected a diabetes subgroup for which the follow-up intention-to-treat estimate is 0.25 with an unadjusted p-value of 0.03. Because subgroup selection was post hoc, no multiplicity adjustment was applied, and no independent replication exists, this result does not support a confirmed heterogeneity claim. It is a hypothesis for a future study that prespecifies the subgroup and analysis. It should not be used to target appointments or to reinterpret the overall experimental result.

The welfare question is broader than any single endpoint in the experiment. Benefits may include completed indicated care, lower travel time and cost, convenience, and possible health improvements. Costs include platform and clinician resources, patient time, public spending, and any care displaced from noninvited patients. Distribution also matters: priority may transfer access toward patients with severe transportation barriers, but the study does not fully observe the circumstances or forgone benefits of those who might be displaced. A social welfare ranking would require valuing these margins, measuring resource costs and clinical quality, and identifying spillovers under capacity constraints. The experiment does none of these completely. It therefore supports neither a claim of positive net social welfare nor a recommendation to expand the platform without qualification.

The paper’s contribution is to keep three policy objects distinct. The first is the causal effect of an invitation on the patients randomized to receive it. For the preregistered high-barrier population, that effect is a 0.150 increase in timely follow-up and a 90-minute reduction in travel. The second is the effect of completing a platform visit. The observed first stage permits a Wald calculation, but the required exclusion and interference assumptions are not secured. The third is the effect of the platform on systemwide access and welfare. Observational spillover patterns and an imprecise clinician-week total show why this object cannot be inferred from the individual intention-to-treat estimate.

These distinctions discipline the practical interpretation. Scarce telemedicine invitations can be an effective way to improve access for selected rural patients facing large travel barriers. Whether the same allocation expands care rather than redistributes it depends on capacity, substitution, and the counterfactual use of clinician time. Whether it improves welfare additionally depends on clinical quality, resource cost, spending incidence, and the value placed on access across patients. The current evidence settles the first proposition within the study population. It leaves the latter two open.
