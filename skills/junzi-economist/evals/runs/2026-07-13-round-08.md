# Round 08 — repeated recovery and cross-disciplinary correction

## Purpose

Move beyond one successful structural sample and test whether medical and institutional evidence can force an upstream economic-model revision.

## JE-X012: repeated structural recovery

The agent predeclared and ran 120 panels: 60 each with 100 and 400 physicians. It preserved both starts and all replication diagnostics, then reported failures, bias, RMSE, empirical dispersion, Hessian standard errors, Wald coverage, boundary hits, worst cases, and sample-size sensitivity. Independent recalculation reproduced every statistic.

The population CCP Jacobian had full column rank at truth, supporting regular local identification under fixed transitions, discounting, shock scale, equilibrium, and model specification. Nearby and broad grids plus 12 inverse searches found no observational equivalence, while the response correctly refused to call this a global proof.

The larger sample reduced RMSE but exposed 88.3% Wald coverage for replacement cost. The result was retained as an inference warning rather than hidden or overgeneralized.

Verdict: `pass`.

Follow-up: validate checkpoint keys before resume and test finite-difference steps before assigning coverage failure to statistical inference.

## JE-X013: clinical evidence revises readmission economics

Primary clinical research and CMS documentation defeated the scalar assumption that 30-day all-cause readmission is a direct monotone quality measure. The response replaced the one-hospital, binary-outcome model with multiple organizations, competing death and return processes, planned/unplanned classification, observation and ED status, risk adjustment, access, coding, selection, heterogeneous transition technology, and a measurement function distinct from welfare.

Independent audit verified the principal CMS correction and sampled clinical claims. The conceptual cross-disciplinary correction passed. The full task remained mixed because its triple-difference equation omitted lower-order interactions and inference details, some feasible channels were promoted beyond direct evidence, and several diagnostic signatures were treated too close to mechanism identification.

Verdict: `mixed`; cross-disciplinary-correction criterion `proved`.

Protocol change: saturate DDD designs, treat mechanism signatures as diagnostic, and separate facts that defeat assumptions from evidence that actors used a feasible response channel.
