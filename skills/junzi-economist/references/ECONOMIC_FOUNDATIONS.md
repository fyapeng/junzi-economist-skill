# Economic foundations — 经济学之法

## Purpose

Use this map to construct the economic explanation before choosing an empirical or computational method. It is a reasoning framework, not a substitute for textbooks or current literature.

## The minimal economic structure

Represent a problem through the relevant subset of:

1. **Agents:** households, workers, firms, providers, governments, platforms, intermediaries, communities, or future generations.
2. **Objectives:** utility, profit, mission, survival, political support, professional norms, or multiple conflicting goals.
3. **Constraints:** budgets, time, capacity, technology, law, geography, health, cognition, credit, information, and organizational authority.
4. **Choices:** consumption, labor, investment, entry, price, quality, location, search, matching, treatment, reporting, or compliance.
5. **Information and beliefs:** private information, signals, learning, expectations, ambiguity, and strategic disclosure.
6. **Interaction:** prices, contracts, games, matching, bargaining, networks, queues, organizations, and political processes.
7. **Outcome concept:** individual choice, partial equilibrium, general equilibrium, dynamic equilibrium, allocation, distribution, or institutional performance.
8. **Evaluation:** efficiency, welfare, incidence, inequality, rights, agency, resilience, and long-run development.

Do not assume stable preferences, full rationality, complete markets, competitive price-taking, or frictionless enforcement without justification.

## Microeconomic foundations

### Choice under constraints

Ask what is chosen, from which feasible set, under which uncertainty and horizon. Distinguish observed behavior from welfare. Preferences may be state-dependent, socially formed, internally conflicting, or only partially revealed.

### Production, cost, and capability

Identify inputs, outputs, quality, capacity, technology, fixed and variable costs, scale and scope, learning, organizational capital, and investment. For hospitals, schools, or public agencies, profit maximization may be an incomplete objective.

### Risk, time, and expectations

Separate risk from ambiguity; current flow payoffs from continuation values; realized shocks from anticipated policy. Examine discounting, insurance, precaution, option value, irreversibility, and belief formation.

### Information, incentives, and contracts

Map who knows and observes what, what can be contracted upon, and how actions affect others. Consider adverse selection, moral hazard, signaling, screening, multitasking, relational contracts, incomplete contracts, and information design.

### Strategic interaction and market organization

Specify timing, actions, payoffs, commitment, entry, and equilibrium. Consider price and nonprice competition, product differentiation, capacity, quality, search, matching, bargaining, auctions, platforms, networks, and market power.

### Equilibrium and welfare

Trace how individual responses alter prices, congestion, entry, quality, fiscal costs, and others' feasible sets. Distinguish local treatment effects from equilibrium policy effects. State the welfare criterion and incidence across groups.

## Macroeconomic foundations

### Aggregation

Explain how heterogeneous agents and sectoral interactions generate aggregates. Avoid treating representative-agent results as distribution-free. Check composition effects, aggregation conditions, and stock-flow consistency.

### Growth and structural transformation

Consider capital, technology, human capital, institutions, demographics, innovation, misallocation, sectoral change, and convergence. Separate level effects, transitional dynamics, and long-run growth.

### Fluctuations, frictions, and propagation

Map shocks, state variables, nominal and real rigidities, financial constraints, labor-market adjustment, inventories, expectations, and amplification. Distinguish impulse, propagation, and measurement.

### Fiscal, monetary, and financial systems

Trace budget constraints, intertemporal incidence, debt, taxation, transfers, money, credit, liquidity, default, and policy rules. Account for expectations and general-equilibrium feedback.

### Open and spatial economies

Include trade costs, migration, capital flows, exchange rates, location, housing, transportation, local public goods, and spatial equilibrium where relevant.

## Institutions, distribution, and power

Markets are embedded in property rights, regulation, enforcement, organizations, political authority, and social norms. Ask who sets rules, controls resources, can exit, bears risk, and is excluded from measured exchange. Efficiency does not settle legitimacy or distribution.

## Theory discipline

- Use the smallest structure that isolates the mechanism.
- State which conclusions follow from assumptions, which from equilibrium, and which are empirical.
- Develop at least one serious rival mechanism.
- Derive discriminating predictions rather than post hoc narratives.
- Treat model failure as information about the abstraction, not merely a reason to add parameters.
- Use mathematical elegance only when it increases clarity, identification, or counterfactual discipline.
