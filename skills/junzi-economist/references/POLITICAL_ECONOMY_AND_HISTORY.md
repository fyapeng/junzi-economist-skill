# Political economy and history — 制度、权力与历史之法

Use this module when economic rules are chosen, contested, unevenly enforced, or transformed. Institutions are mechanisms and outcomes, not fixed background variables.

## Material and social relations

Locate production, ownership, control, labor, exchange, reproduction, and distribution in concrete social relations. Ask who controls productive resources, information, organization, and legitimate coercion; who performs work; who appropriates surplus or bears loss; and which alternatives are practically available.

Use class, bargaining power, organizational authority, status, gender, household relations, professional norms, and collective action when they alter feasible choices or distribution. Do not force all conflict into prices or all institutions into individual preference.

## Rules, implementation, and feedback

Separate formal rule, implementation, compliance, evasion, adjudication, and lived effect. Identify who proposes, decides, funds, administers, monitors, interprets, contests, and can exit. Administrative capacity and local adaptation can reverse the mechanism implied by statutory text.

Policies reshape interests, organizations, information, and future coalitions. Treat policy feedback and endogenous rule change as possible dynamics rather than assuming invariant institutions.

## Contradiction and structural change

Look for tensions among productive capacity, ownership, incentives, coordination, distribution, legitimacy, and ecological or social reproduction. A contradiction is useful only when its opposing forces, observable manifestations, and possible transformations are specified.

Distinguish marginal adjustment within a structure from change in the structure itself. Entry, innovation, crisis, collective action, legal change, and political reorganization may alter action sets, property rights, market boundaries, or equilibrium concepts, making old parameters policy-dependent.

## Historical investigation

Reconstruct sequence, alternatives, and path dependence. Avoid reading present categories backward or treating the observed institution as inevitable. Compare periods and places only after checking meaning, measurement, enforcement, technology, and political coalition.

Historical material can identify mechanisms through process evidence, institutional discontinuities, rejected alternatives, and changing constraints. Detail alone does not establish causality, and lack of a conventional regression does not make it irrelevant.

## Public action and mass viewpoint

Investigate how policies operate for affected people, especially those absent from administrative data or formal voice. Local knowledge and lived experience can reveal hidden constraints, implementation failure, and unmeasured outcomes. Participation is evidence and democratic practice, not an automatic guarantee of representativeness or truth.

Combine investigation with independent analysis: neither official description, expert authority, market outcome, nor popular belief settles the mechanism by itself.

## Political-economy stopping test

Before treating an institution as an exogenous treatment or fixed constraint, state its historical formation; actors, interests, authority, and resources; formal rule versus implementation; enforcement, evasion, and capacity; distribution of gains, losses, risk, and voice; policy feedback; plausible unrealized alternatives; and evidence that would defeat the mechanism.
