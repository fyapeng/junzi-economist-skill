# Source map — 理论谱系与使用边界

Last bibliographic audit: 2026-07-13.

This map supports lineage, theoretical-balance, and study-planning tasks. For a narrow citation or quotation check, load `SOURCE_PROTOCOL.md` instead.

## Marxism and Mao Zedong Thought

### Marx

- Karl Marx, *A Contribution to the Critique of Political Economy* (1859), especially the Preface and 1857 Introduction: production, distribution, exchange, historically specific categories, relations of production, and structural change.
- Karl Marx, *Capital: A Critique of Political Economy*, Vol. I (1867), Vol. II (1885), Vol. III (1894): commodity and value forms, labor process, accumulation, circulation and reproduction, competition, profit, credit, rent, and crisis tendencies.
- Karl Marx, *The Eighteenth Brumaire of Louis Bonaparte* (1852): historically situated agency, class coalitions, political representation, and non-mechanical relations between material interests and political outcomes.

Use these works to ask how economic categories express social relations, how production and reproduction are organized, and how accumulation, ownership, labor, power, and historical change interact. Do not reduce them to moral slogans or treat every contemporary empirical margin as deducible from nineteenth-century categories.

### Mao Zedong

- “Oppose Book Worship” (1930): investigate present facts and history before prescribing a solution.
- “On Practice” (1937): knowledge develops through the practice–knowledge–practice cycle; both dogmatism and narrow empiricism fail.
- “On Contradiction” (1937): analyze the particular contradiction, its principal aspect, and changes across stages.
- “Reform Our Study” (1941): join theory with systematic investigation of concrete economic, political, and historical conditions.

Use these texts primarily in Dao, Shi, branch revision, and the relation between theory and evidence. They do not supply an estimator or license predetermined political conclusions.

Primary-text checks: [Marx, *Capital* Vol. I](https://www.marxists.org/archive/marx/works/1867-c1/), [Marx, 1859 Preface](https://www.marxists.org/archive/marx/works/1859/critique-pol-economy/preface.htm), [Mao, “On Practice”](https://www.marxists.org/reference/archive/mao/selected-works/volume-1/mswv1_16.htm), [Mao, “On Contradiction”](https://www.marxists.org/reference/archive/mao/selected-works/volume-1/mswv1_17.htm), and [Mao, “Oppose Book Worship”](https://www.marxists.org/reference/archive/mao/selected-works/volume-6/mswv6_11.htm).

## Microeconomic foundations

### General theory

- Andreu Mas-Colell, Michael D. Whinston, and Jerry R. Green, *Microeconomic Theory* (Oxford University Press, 1995): choice, production, uncertainty, game theory, information, general equilibrium, and welfare. Treat it as the main graduate-level coverage audit for `MICROECONOMIC_LAW.md`.
- David M. Kreps, *Microeconomic Foundations I: Choice and Competitive Markets* (Princeton University Press, 2013): foundations and limits of choice and competitive-market representations.
- Ariel Rubinstein, *Lecture Notes in Microeconomic Theory: The Economic Agent* (Princeton University Press, 2nd ed., 2012): explicit modeling language and critical examination of the economic agent.

### Games, information, contracts, and organization

- Drew Fudenberg and Jean Tirole, *Game Theory* (MIT Press, 1991): static and dynamic strategic interaction, information, and equilibrium refinement.
- Patrick Bolton and Mathias Dewatripont, *Contract Theory* (MIT Press, 2005): information, incentives, incomplete contracting, and organizational design.
- Paul Milgrom and John Roberts, *Economics, Organization and Management* (Prentice Hall, 1992): organizations, complementarities, incentives, and coordination.

### Industrial organization and market design

- Jean Tirole, *The Theory of Industrial Organization* (MIT Press, 1988): conduct, entry, product differentiation, information, and strategic investment.
- Paul Klemperer, *Auctions: Theory and Practice* (Princeton University Press, 2004), and Alvin E. Roth and Marilda A. Oliveira Sotomayor, *Two-Sided Matching* (Cambridge University Press, 1990): auction and matching institutions.

Use field texts after defining agents, timing, information, market boundaries, and welfare. A named model is a candidate abstraction, not the research question.

## Macroeconomic foundations

### Dynamic methods and equilibrium

- Nancy L. Stokey, Robert E. Lucas Jr., with Edward C. Prescott, *Recursive Methods in Economic Dynamics* (Harvard University Press, 1989): dynamic programming, recursive competitive equilibrium, and stochastic dynamics.
- Lars Ljungqvist and Thomas J. Sargent, *Recursive Macroeconomic Theory*, 4th ed. (MIT Press, 2018): recursive methods across labor, fiscal, monetary, asset-pricing, heterogeneous-agent, and policy problems.
- David Romer, *Advanced Macroeconomics*, 5th ed. (McGraw-Hill, 2019): a broad bridge across growth, fluctuations, labor, and policy.

### Growth, fluctuations, money, and heterogeneity

- Daron Acemoglu, *Introduction to Modern Economic Growth* (Princeton University Press, 2009): growth, technology, institutions, political economy, and structural transformation.
- Michael Woodford, *Interest and Prices* (Princeton University Press, 2003), and Jordi Galí, *Monetary Policy, Inflation, and the Business Cycle*, 2nd ed. (Princeton University Press, 2015): nominal rigidities, expectations, and monetary policy.
- Truman Bewley, *Why Wages Don't Fall During a Recession* (Harvard University Press, 1999), and canonical heterogeneous-agent papers by S. Rao Aiyagari (1994) and Mark Huggett (1993): institutional evidence and distribution beyond representative-agent closure.

Always begin with accounting, balance sheets, aggregation, regime, and horizon. Recursive tools belong to Shu when they solve a model; the economic closure they implement belongs to Fa.

## Institutions, political economy, and human development

- Torsten Persson and Guido Tabellini, *Political Economics: Explaining Economic Policy* (MIT Press, 2000): voting, representation, redistribution, commitment, and policy formation.
- Daron Acemoglu and James A. Robinson, *Economic Origins of Dictatorship and Democracy* (Cambridge University Press, 2006): political transitions, de facto and de jure power, and institutional dynamics.
- Elinor Ostrom, *Governing the Commons* (Cambridge University Press, 1990): collective governance beyond a market–state dichotomy.
- Karl Polanyi, *The Great Transformation* (1944): markets as historically instituted processes and social countermovements.
- Amartya Sen, *Commodities and Capabilities* (1985), *Collective Choice and Social Welfare* (1970; expanded ed. 2017), and *Development as Freedom* (1999): capabilities, social choice, agency, and informational limits of welfare metrics.

Use these as rival and complementary accounts. Do not collapse Marxian production relations, rational-choice political economy, institutional analysis, and capability theory into one vocabulary. State where their objects, mechanisms, and normative commitments differ.

## Econometrics, causal inference, and structural work

- Fumio Hayashi, *Econometrics* (Princeton University Press, 2000), Bruce E. Hansen, *Econometrics* (2022), and Jeffrey M. Wooldridge, *Econometric Analysis of Cross Section and Panel Data*, 2nd ed. (MIT Press, 2010): probability, estimation, identification, and inference.
- Guido W. Imbens and Donald B. Rubin, *Causal Inference* (Cambridge University Press, 2015), and Miguel Hernán and James Robins, *Causal Inference: What If* (2020): potential outcomes, assignment, longitudinal treatment, and target-trial reasoning.
- Daniel McFadden’s discrete-choice work, Kenneth Train’s *Discrete Choice Methods with Simulation* (2nd ed., 2009), Steven Berry, James Levinsohn, and Ariel Pakes (1995), and John Rust (1987): choice, demand, dynamic decisions, identification, estimation, and counterfactuals.
- Kenneth L. Judd, *Numerical Methods in Economics* (MIT Press, 1998; paperback 2023): approximation, optimization, integration, dynamic programming, and computational equilibrium.

These sources chiefly inform Shu. They cannot create the economic object, prove assumptions from convention, or promote exploratory results to confirmation.

## Materials useful for a later audit

User-provided copies are most valuable when exact chapter alignment, notation, exercises, or page citations are needed. Priority order:

1. MWG or another graduate micro sequence actually used by the researcher;
2. the preferred graduate macro sequence, especially dynamic and heterogeneous-agent material;
3. field texts for the researcher’s main domains, such as IO, health, public, labor, spatial, or development;
4. Chinese institutional and political-economy teaching materials whose terminology should be represented accurately.

Do not ingest whole books into runtime context. Build compact chapter-to-question indexes after inspecting lawful user-provided copies.
